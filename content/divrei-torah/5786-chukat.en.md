The parsha opens with the law of the red heifer. It's one of the strangest laws in the Torah, and the rabbis have always singled it out.

A perfect red heifer—one without blemish, one that has never had a yoke on it—is brought outside the camp and slaughtered. Its blood is sprinkled toward the Tent of Meeting. The whole animal is burned. The ashes are gathered. And those ashes, mixed with water, become the substance used to purify someone who has come into contact with death.

So far, maybe that's comprehensible. A purification rite.

But here's the strange part. The priest who handles the red heifer to make the purifying ashes—that priest becomes impure. The people who receive the ashes-water become pure. The same substance that purifies the impure also makes the pure impure.

The ashes purify the defiled, and defile the pure.

The rabbis say: this law is beyond human reason. *Chok*—a decree without explanation. The Torah gives it to us and says, essentially, *you can't understand this one*. Even Solomon, the wisest of men, said: *I thought I could become wise, but it was far from me*. He was referring to the red heifer.

Why would the Torah include a law it tells us we can't understand?

Maybe because the Torah is being honest about something. Some parts of moral and spiritual reality aren't fully comprehensible. Some things operate on logic we don't fully have access to. And pretending we understand them—forcing everything into a rational frame—actually cuts us off from a deeper truth.

The red heifer is a reminder that the universe contains mysteries. That not everything we're called to do will make sense to us. That the structure of what's holy and what's defiled isn't always transparent.

This is hard for a modern reader. We're trained to demand explanations. Why should I do this? What's the logic? What's the benefit? Give me the reason.

And most of the time, the Torah gives reasons. Keep Shabbat because God created the world in six days and rested on the seventh. Don't oppress the stranger because you were strangers in Egypt. The commandments come with context.

But the red heifer sits there as an exception. No context. No reason you can hold. Just: *this is how it works*.

The rabbis call laws like this *chukim*. Decrees. They distinguish them from *mishpatim*—laws whose reason we can see. Don't steal, don't murder, don't oppress—those make ethical sense. But don't wear wool and linen mixed, don't boil a kid in its mother's milk, don't mix plant species in your field—these have no obvious reason. They're chukim.

A religious life, the rabbis suggest, includes both. The rational commandments and the non-rational ones. And doing the non-rational ones teaches something the rational ones can't: that obedience isn't contingent on comprehension.

There's a deeper point here, too. The red heifer deals with death. It's the substance that lets you come back into the community after contact with death. And death—truly—is the great mystery. We don't understand it. We can't explain it. We can describe it, but we can't make it make sense.

So maybe the law being incomprehensible fits the subject. The Torah doesn't pretend to explain death. It gives us a ritual to reconnect the living to the community after contact with the inexplicable.

And perhaps that's the deepest teaching of the red heifer: there are parts of life where our role isn't to understand, but to participate. To do the ritual. To trust the process. To accept that some things remain opaque even while we do them.

## What We Carry Forward

There are things in your life that don't make sense. Don't force them to make sense. Some of the deepest experiences—grief, love, faith, fear—aren't comprehensible, and trying to reason them into comprehensibility actually destroys what they are. This week, let something remain mysterious. Participate in it anyway.
