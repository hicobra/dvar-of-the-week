Yaakov is about to meet Esav after twenty years. He's terrified. He sends his family across the river. And then, alone in the dark, a man appears and wrestles with him all night.

> **וַיֵּאָבֵק אִישׁ עִמּוֹ עַד עֲלוֹת הַשָּׁחַר** &middot; *Vayei'avek ish imo ad alot hashachar* &middot; "And a man wrestled with him until the break of dawn" (Bereishit 32:25).

Yaakov holds his own. The "man" — Chazal say he was a malach, an angel — can't beat him. As the sun is about to rise, the angel touches Yaakov's hip, dislocating it. Then Yaakov makes him bless him. And the angel gives him a new name: Yisrael.

So Yaakov wins. He gets the blessing. He gets the new name. But he also leaves this encounter with something else: he limps. The Torah tells us he limps for the rest of his life.

The limp bothers me. Why does Yaakov have to carry a wound out of a fight he won?

I think it's because the Torah wants us to understand what a real struggle actually costs.

The small fights you have with your life leave no trace. You forget them in a week. But the real ones — the ones that change who you are, the ones that earn you a new name — those you don't walk away from unchanged. You come out of them limping.

The limp isn't a consolation prize. It's the proof. It's the evidence that the wrestling was real. An angel that leaves you perfectly fine was never wrestling you in the first place. A night of real struggle changes how you walk afterward.

This is a strange kind of comfort, if you've ever come through a hard thing and found yourself not quite the same as before. A relationship that almost ended. A year of loss. A faith that got tested. You don't emerge from those the way you went in. You limp a little. You favor one side. Something reminds you it happened, every day.

The Torah is saying: that's not damage. That's the new name. The limp and the name come together.

## Takeaway

If you've come out of a hard stretch walking a little differently — that's not something to fix. That's how you can tell it was real. Yaakov limped his whole life. And he was also Yisrael.
