Tazria and Metzora are difficult parshiot to sit with. They deal with *tzaraat*—a skin condition that the Torah treats with a complex system of inspection, isolation, and eventual purification. Modern translations usually render it as "leprosy," though the medical reality is probably different.

But the Torah doesn't treat it as a medical problem to be solved. It treats it as a status to be managed and eventually redeemed.

The person with tzaraat must live outside the camp, separated from the community. They must announce themselves: "Unclean, unclean." They must live in isolation until—if—they're pronounced clean by the priest. And even then, there's an elaborate purification process before they can rejoin.

It's easy to read this as primitive superstition: a ritual for a disease. But the Torah isn't primarily concerned with the disease. It's concerned with how a community treats someone who is separated from it.

The word *tazria* literally means "to implant" or "to seed." The parsha opens with the laws of purity following childbirth—a woman who has given birth is in a state of ritual separation. And from this beginning, we move into the laws of tzaraat. Something is being *implanted* in us, the parsha suggests, through these experiences of separation and return.

Here's what might be happening: The person with tzaraat is separated not because they're dangerous to others, but because separation itself is purifying. It creates a boundary. It says: *This state is different. You are different, temporarily.* The separation isn't punishment; it's acknowledgment. You can't return to the community pretending nothing changed. You have to be publicly recognized as changed, then go through a process of reintegration.

We try not to do this anymore. We try to hide what separates us. We show up to work or community looking fine, even when internally we're living in isolation. We present the outward semblance of belonging while experiencing separation. It's more efficient. It's more polite.

But the Torah suggests something different: that sometimes isolation needs to be acknowledged. That the work of healing isn't invisible. That coming back to community after separation requires a public process, a ritual, a priest who says: *Yes, you're clean now. Yes, you can return.* And the community accepts the pronouncement.

The elaborate nature of the purification process—the birds, the water, the waiting—isn't about the medical reality. It's about re-establishing the person's place in the community. It's a ceremony of reintegration. It says: *Your separation was real. Your return is also real. We're witnessing it.*

We don't have priests anymore, but we have moments when we need someone to mark the boundary, to acknowledge the separation, and to pronounce us clean again. A therapist. A friend who simply says, "I notice you've been different, and I'm glad you're here again." A community that accepts your return not as if nothing happened, but with the understanding that you've been somewhere and now you're back.

The person with tzaraat doesn't get to skip the separation or the purification. They have to live it fully. And then, and only then, they can return. The process is public, witnessed, real.

## What We Carry Forward

If you're in a season of separation right now—whether by circumstance or illness or change—the Torah says: that separation is real. You can't pretend it's not happening. And when you're ready to return, you'll need more than just showing up. You'll need someone to witness your return. Don't skip that ritual. It's how you become clean again.
