The parsha opens with a law about vows.

> **When a man makes a vow to the Lord or swears an oath to bind himself by a pledge, he shall not break his word; he shall do according to all that proceeds out of his mouth.** (Numbers 30:3)

The Hebrew is stark. *Lo yachel dvaro*. He shall not profane his word. Shall not make it hollow. Shall not empty it out.

A word given, the Torah says, is a thing with weight. To break it is to profane it—to desecrate something that should be holy.

We live in a time when words are cheap. We send hundreds of them every day. We promise, suggest, commit, cancel. We say we'll call and don't. We say we're interested and aren't. We say yes when we mean maybe. We say we'll be there and show up late or not at all.

The parsha doesn't accept this. The parsha says: your word, once given, binds you. Not because society enforces it. Not because there's a contract. But because *something* of you is in the word you spoke. And to break it is to break something of yourself.

There are specific categories discussed. A man's vow. A woman's vow. A daughter's vow that can be annulled by her father if he objects on the day he hears it. A wife's vow that can be annulled by her husband in the same way. A widow's or divorced woman's vow—which stands, regardless.

The details matter less, for my purposes, than the underlying assumption: vows are serious. Vows bind. And the Torah wants us to know how seriously it takes the words we say.

The Mishna opens its tractate on vows with a warning, essentially. *It is better not to vow at all than to vow and not fulfill*. The rabbis were anxious about how easily people make commitments they don't keep. They understood something about human nature—we say more than we can do. We promise more than we can deliver. We use words loosely and then are surprised when the words don't bear the weight we put on them.

The Torah's solution isn't to refuse to speak. It's to speak carefully. To treat the act of committing as a sacred act. To know that when you say "I will," something changes in the fabric of your obligations.

This has implications in small ways and large. When you tell your child "I'll be there at pickup time," that's a vow. When you tell a colleague "I'll have it done by Friday," that's a vow. When you tell a friend "I'll call you this weekend," that's a vow. Not in the formal religious sense—but in the sense the parsha means.

And if you don't do what you said, something is profaned. Not dramatically. Not catastrophically. But a little. Your word becomes less weighty. The people around you trust it less. You, yourself, come to know that your word is more aspirational than binding.

Over time, this accumulates. A person whose word cannot be trusted becomes unreliable in a deep way—not just in specific instances, but in their overall presence in the world. They become someone whose statements float rather than land.

The Torah offers a different vision. A person whose word binds. A person whose "yes" means yes and whose "no" means no. A person you can count on, because their words correspond to reality.

This is a kind of integrity—in the literal sense. The word means "wholeness." Someone whose words and actions are integrated. Someone who doesn't split between what they say and what they do.

I think most of us want to be this kind of person. We don't set out to break our word. But we say yes to things casually, and then find ourselves overcommitted, and then have to break something.

The parsha's warning is worth hearing. Say less. Mean more of what you say. Don't profane the word.

## What We Carry Forward

Something you said this week, you haven't done yet. Go do it. Or go say you won't. But don't just let it dissolve. Small words matter. Your integrity is built, one kept word at a time.
