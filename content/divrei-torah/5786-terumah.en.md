God tells Moshe to build a Mishkan. And when He explains why, He uses a word that's slightly off from what you'd expect.

> **וְעָשׂוּ לִי מִקְדָּשׁ וְשָׁכַנְתִּי בְּתוֹכָם** &middot; "And they shall make for Me a sanctuary, and I will dwell among them" (Shemot 25:8).

The word that catches your eye, if you read Hebrew, is *b'tocham* — "among them." Not *b'tocho*, "in it." You'd expect: *make Me a sanctuary and I will dwell in it*. That would be the natural grammar — the sanctuary is what you just built, so "in it" makes sense.

The Torah says: *I will dwell among them.*

The Chassidic masters and many others read this literally. The dwelling place God is asking for is not the building. The building is the occasion. The dwelling happens in the people who make it.

Take a second with that. The Mishkan is gold-plated, beautifully made, holy, central, the focal point of the entire camp. And the Torah is saying, at the very moment it's being introduced: don't confuse the container for the thing. The presence is not in the wood and the gold. It's in you.

There's a reason this is quietly revolutionary. A lot of religion — and a lot of life — is the mistake of confusing the holy building for the holy thing. We can build beautiful institutions, structures, communities, careers, and miss that the purpose of all of them was to be a vessel for a presence that lives somewhere else entirely.

The Torah is not saying don't build the Mishkan. It *commanded* the Mishkan. The Mishkan is crucial. But in the same breath it commands it, the Torah corrects what would have been the automatic misunderstanding: this is not where I live. I live among them. The Mishkan is the invitation for you to hold yourselves the way I asked this structure to be held — with intention, with care, with light inside.

This reframes a lot. When a community feels hollow, the answer isn't usually to make the building more beautiful. When a life feels spiritually empty, it's rarely solved by adding more structure on the outside. The question comes back, inevitably, to the same place: is there something dwelling within?

## Takeaway

The Torah doesn't say "I will dwell in it." It says "I will dwell among them." Whatever you're building this week — a home, a project, a community — the building is the occasion. The presence has to live somewhere else. Somewhere closer.
