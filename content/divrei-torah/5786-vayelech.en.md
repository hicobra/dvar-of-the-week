The parsha is short. One of the shortest in the Torah. And in it, Moses does something hard.

He hands over.

> **I am a hundred and twenty years old today. I can no longer come and go. And the Lord has said to me, 'You shall not cross this Jordan.'** (Deuteronomy 31:2)

Moses knows. He knows he's at the end. And he does what a leader who knows how to end does: he prepares the transition.

He calls Joshua. He tells him, in front of the whole people:

> **Be strong and courageous, for you shall go with this people into the land.** (Deuteronomy 31:7)

*Chazak v'ematz.* Be strong and courageous.

This phrase will be repeated, with variations, three times in this transition scene. Moses says it to Joshua. God says it to Joshua. It's the core teaching of the handoff.

Why strength? Why courage?

Because what Joshua is about to do is hard. Not the military part. The part of inheriting something that's not originally yours. The part of standing where someone else stood. The part of leading people who remember the one who led before you, and comparing you to him, sometimes unfavorably, for years.

Joshua has been Moses's assistant for forty years. He has seen Moses up close. He knows Moses is irreplaceable. And now Moses is saying: *you will lead*.

That requires strength. Not just physical strength. The strength to accept the position. The strength not to flinch. The strength to lead *as Joshua*, not as a pale imitation of Moses.

And it requires courage. Because Joshua will fail at times. He will make decisions Moses would have made differently. He will be compared. And he has to keep going.

The parsha is tender, in its way. Moses doesn't resent the transition. He doesn't try to cling to power past his time. He doesn't try to control Joshua from beyond. He blesses him, and lets him go.

This is worth pausing on. How many leaders—of countries, of organizations, of families—actually manage to hand over gracefully? Many don't. Many try to hang on past their prime. Many try to sabotage their successors, subtly or openly, so that they'll look indispensable in comparison. Many cling to what they've built and refuse to release it.

Moses doesn't. He sets up the transition publicly. He names Joshua in front of everyone. He charges Joshua with specific tasks. And he steps back.

Then he does one more thing. He writes the Torah.

> **Moses wrote this Torah and gave it to the priests, the sons of Levi, who carried the Ark of the Covenant of the Lord, and to all the elders of Israel.** (Deuteronomy 31:9)

Moses, knowing he's about to die, writes down what he's been teaching. He puts it in a form that will outlast him. He gives it to the custodians of sacred memory. He ensures that what he has been teaching orally, for forty years, will continue to be available after he's gone.

This is how Moses handles his own end. He hands over the leadership. He writes down the teaching. He makes sure that the work will continue without him.

The parsha ends with God telling Moses that he should teach the Israelites a song—a song that will testify against them when they go astray. A song that will be remembered, not as Moses's, but as the song of the people. A way for the teaching to live in their mouths, to be passed from generation to generation, even without a specific teacher.

Moses is, in effect, distributing himself. Not clinging to his own centrality. Making himself less necessary, so that what he has built can go on without him.

This is, I think, a profound model for how to end things well. Whether a leadership role, a project, a relationship, or a life. You prepare the next steward. You write down what needs to be remembered. You teach the song. You let go.

And the core charge to those who come after you is simple. *Be strong and courageous.*

## What We Carry Forward

Something in your life is due to be handed over. A responsibility. A role. A thing you've been holding too long. This week, start preparing the handoff. Name who comes next. Write down what they need to know. Bless them. Let go.
