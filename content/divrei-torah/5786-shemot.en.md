Moshe is shepherding sheep in the desert, way out by Chorev. He's not looking for God. He's looking for grass for the flock. And then he sees something strange out of the corner of his eye.

> **וַיַּרְא וְהִנֵּה הַסְּנֶה בֹּעֵר בָּאֵשׁ וְהַסְּנֶה אֵינֶנּוּ אֻכָּל... וַיֹּאמֶר מֹשֶׁה אָסֻרָה־נָּא וְאֶרְאֶה אֶת־הַמַּרְאֶה הַגָּדֹל הַזֶּה** &middot; "He saw — behold, the bush was burning in fire and the bush was not being consumed... And Moshe said, 'Let me turn aside and see this great sight'" (Shemot 3:2–3).

There's a small detail in the next verse that I always miss. The Torah says *vayar Hashem ki sar lirot* — "And God saw that he turned aside to look" — and *only then* God calls out to him.

Think about that sequence. The bush is burning. Moshe is walking past. Only when Moshe *turns aside* — only when he deliberately stops what he's doing and goes closer to look — does God speak.

If Moshe had kept walking, we don't know what would have happened. The Torah doesn't say the bush would have chased him down. It doesn't say God would have called out regardless. The Torah quietly implies: the revelation was waiting for him to notice.

This is a slightly uncomfortable thought. We like to imagine God grabs us when needed. The Torah here shows a different pattern. The burning bush was already there. It was on fire before Moshe saw it. It might have been on fire for a while. What made the encounter an encounter was one thing — Moshe turned aside.

There's a lesson in that small verb. Most of the things that matter most in life show up in the margins of our attention. They're burning on the side of the path while we're busy getting to somewhere. And whether they become a moment of meaning or a background detail depends, almost entirely, on whether we turn aside.

You can walk past a burning bush. Plenty of people do. The Torah isn't saying "don't." It's saying, more honestly: here's what happens when you do.

## Takeaway

Something is probably burning on the side of your path this week. A person who needs a moment, a thought that won't leave you, a small pull you can't name. The first thing Moshe does — before any miracle, before any speech — is turn aside. That's still the whole move.
