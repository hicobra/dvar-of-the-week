Korach leads a rebellion against Moses. He gathers two hundred and fifty leaders—respected men, recognized voices in the community—and they come to Moses and say, essentially: *Who do you think you are? Why should you be in charge? We're all holy. The whole congregation is holy. Why have you elevated yourself above us?*

The argument sounds compelling. The whole community *is* holy. God said so explicitly. So why should Moses and Aaron be in a different category?

The rabbis spend a lot of time on this rebellion. They ask: what was actually wrong with what Korach said? Because on the surface, it sounds reasonable. It even sounds spiritual—an appeal to equality and shared holiness.

The answer they give has become famous. It's in the Ethics of the Fathers. *A dispute for the sake of Heaven will endure; one that is not for the sake of Heaven will not endure. What is a dispute for the sake of Heaven? Hillel and Shammai. And one that is not for the sake of Heaven? Korach and his assembly.*

Notice the asymmetry. Hillel and Shammai are named as the two parties to a holy dispute. But Korach isn't paired with Moses. He's paired with *his own assembly*. The implication being: Korach wasn't really arguing with Moses. He was organizing his own power base.

The rabbis are saying: disputes that are genuinely about principles—that's what Hillel and Shammai did for decades, arguing about the details of law, each trying to understand the truth more fully—those disputes endure because both sides are pursuing something larger than themselves.

But Korach's dispute wasn't really about shared holiness. That was the frame. The actual subject was power. Korach wanted Moses's position. The appeal to equality was a strategy, not a principle.

And you can tell the difference. Because Korach's argument, while logically sound on the surface, didn't actually advance any cause. It didn't produce wisdom. It didn't clarify anything. It just agitated for power. And when the power didn't come to him, the whole movement collapsed—dramatically, in this case, with the earth opening and swallowing Korach and his closest followers.

This is a practical teaching. When you're in a dispute, at work or in a community or in a family, it's worth asking: is this really about the principle we're fighting about? Or is there something else going on?

Sometimes the answer is: this is genuinely about the thing. We're arguing because we actually see the situation differently, and we both care about what's right.

And sometimes the answer is: no. The fight is a proxy for something else. Usually power, position, recognition, or pride.

Korach-style disputes have a telltale sign. They escalate. They can't be resolved by compromise. They require one party to win. They feel, for some reason, existential.

Hillel-and-Shammai-style disputes have a different sign. They deepen. They produce learning. They make both sides think harder. They can be held, over years, without the people involved becoming enemies.

The Torah isn't saying you shouldn't disagree. It's saying: know why you're disagreeing. If the fight is for the sake of understanding—for the sake of Heaven, in the rabbis' phrase—then it's worth having. If the fight is for the sake of power, it will consume everyone involved, including you.

Moses doesn't answer Korach's argument on its own terms. He doesn't defend his position. He essentially says: *God will decide*. And then the ground opens.

The dispute that isn't for Heaven, in the end, gets resolved by being swallowed up. It doesn't leave anything behind. That's maybe the clearest sign that a fight isn't worth having.

## What We Carry Forward

You're in some disagreement right now. Before you press further, ask yourself: what is this actually about? If it's really about the principle, go deeper. If it's about something else—power, pride, position—notice that. Don't be Korach. The disputes that matter are the ones that produce something, not the ones that just consume people.
