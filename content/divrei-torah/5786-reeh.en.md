The parsha opens with a command that is itself strange.

> **See, I place before you today blessing and curse.** (Deuteronomy 11:26)

*Re'eh.* See. Look. The whole parsha is framed as something to *see*.

But what is there to see? Blessings and curses aren't visible. You can't look at them. They're not objects in the world. They're consequences of choices that haven't been made yet.

So what does Moses mean when he says *see*?

I think he means: *notice that there's a choice*. Notice that you're standing at a fork. Notice that one path leads one way and another leads another. Don't sleepwalk through your life as if the path is just happening to you. *See*—really see—that you're choosing.

This is harder than it sounds. Most of the time we don't feel like we're choosing. We feel like we're reacting. Things happen, and we respond. The environment pushes us, and we push back. We're caught in currents that feel larger than us.

And some of that is true. There are circumstances we don't control. There are histories, biologies, systems that shape what's possible.

But Re'eh is insisting on something anyway. Within whatever constraints you face, there is still a choice. Blessing or curse. One direction or another. The choice is real.

And the first step in making the choice well is *seeing* that you're making it.

The parsha continues with a long list of specific laws. How to set up the worship of God in the land. How to handle false prophets. How to manage the year of release. How to observe the pilgrimage festivals.

But the framing is the opening command. See. Look. Notice.

I think of this in terms of how automatic most of our lives become. We wake up, we do the thing we always do, we respond to the messages, we go through the day. We aren't seeing. We're running a program.

Sometimes a disruption wakes us up. A health scare. A loss. A major decision. Suddenly we see that we're choosing. We notice that there's a fork. We realize the automaticity was itself a choice we were making without acknowledging.

Moses is trying to get the people to see *before* the disruption. Before they've already made choices that turn out badly. He's saying: I'm telling you now, at the beginning, before you go in—there's a choice here. Blessing or curse. See it.

This is a generous teaching, actually. It's not fatalistic. It's not saying "you're either blessed or cursed." It's saying: you're choosing. The blessing or the curse follows from the choice.

You get to decide.

But only if you see that you get to decide.

The ones who can't see—the ones who assume they're just victims of circumstance—end up with whatever happens, uninvolved in their own story. And what happens, for those people, tends toward the curse side of the equation. Not because they chose wrongly, but because they never really *chose*.

The blessing comes to those who see.

And the seeing itself is a gift. Most of us don't have it, most of the time. We drift. Then something jolts us awake and we see. And then we drift again.

The practice of the parsha is to keep seeing. To refuse to go back to sleep. To stand, every day if you can, at the fork, and look at it, and notice that you're choosing.

## What We Carry Forward

Right now, today, there's a choice in front of you. It might not feel dramatic. It might be small. But you're choosing—whether you see it or not. This week, try to see at least one of your choices clearly. Not after you've made it. Before.
