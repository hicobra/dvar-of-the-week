The parsha opens with a title: Acharei-Mot. "After death." After the death of Aaron's sons, which we learned about in Shemini. Now—*after*—comes instruction.

There's something important in the placement. The parsha doesn't theorize about death or grief or tragedy. It doesn't provide comfort. It simply notes that something happened—Aaron's sons died—and then it moves forward with the work that still needs to be done.

Aaron is not told, "Take time. Grieve. You'll be okay." Aaron is told: *Here's what you do now.*

The parsha teaches the laws of Yom Kippur, the Day of Atonement. It's the one day of the year when the high priest enters the Holy of Holies, when he stands closest to God, when the most intimate and crucial act of atonement happens. And it comes after Aaron's devastation.

The work of atonement—the work of standing before God, of mediating for the community, of bringing reconciliation—happens not before grief, but after. Not instead of grief, but in the midst of it.

There's a form of atheism that says: if God were real, there would be no tragedy. If God were real, the children of priests wouldn't die. If God existed, we wouldn't have to keep going and doing the work anyway.

But the Torah presents a different argument: the work *is* the answer to tragedy. Not the answer that makes tragedy sensible. Not the answer that explains why bad things happen. But the answer that says: *Now you keep going. Now you do what you're called to do.*

Aaron brings his sons to offer what they think should be offered, and the fire consumes them. Immediately after this horror, Aaron is told to continue serving. Not to recover. Not to understand. To serve.

The Yom Kippur service that comes after this death is the most solemn and intensive service in the whole year. It's specifically the priest entering alone into the holiest place. It's the place where Aaron would be closest to the presence of God, to meaning, to what matters most. And he does this work, every year, carrying the memory of his sons' deaths.

The parsha doesn't tell us Aaron is transformed by this. It doesn't say the grief is resolved. It just says: *After death, here's what you do.*

We tend to think that grief and function are incompatible. That if you're really grieving, you should stop working, stop showing up, stop pretending. And sometimes that's true—sometimes the grief is so fresh that function isn't possible.

But there's also a form of healing that happens in the doing. Not in getting over it. Not in moving on. But in continuing the work anyway, holding the grief and the function together. Aaron bringing the Yom Kippur offering while carrying the weight of his sons' deaths. The ritual didn't erase what happened. But the ritual continued.

This is something modern psychology is only recently rediscovering: that sometimes, the way through grief is not around it, but through the practices and commitments you already had. The person who returns to their work, their community, their ritual, their relationships—not as a distraction from grief, but as a place where grief can be held and carried.

The high priest, of all people, would know this. The moment when you're closest to God is not the moment of comfort or clarity. It's the moment of greatest solemnity. It's the moment when you're aware of everything you've lost. And you stand there anyway. You do the work. You make the offering. You serve.

## What We Carry Forward

After loss, you don't get to stop. Your particular vocation or commitment doesn't pause. You bring your grief with you into the ordinary work. You do the thing you're called to do, holding both the loss and the calling. This is how you learn that both are real.
