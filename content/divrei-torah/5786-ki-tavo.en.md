The parsha opens with a ritual that sounds almost theatrical.

A farmer, in the land of Israel, takes his first fruits—the very first produce of the year—and brings them to the priest. He puts them in a basket. He brings them to the Temple. And then, before he hands them over, he *speaks*.

Not a blessing. Not a prayer, exactly. He tells a story.

> **My father was a wandering Aramean. He went down to Egypt and sojourned there, few in number, and there he became a great nation, mighty and numerous. The Egyptians dealt harshly with us, and oppressed us, and imposed hard labor upon us. And we cried out to the Lord, the God of our fathers, and the Lord heard our voice, and saw our affliction, and our toil, and our oppression. And the Lord brought us out of Egypt with a mighty hand, and with an outstretched arm... And now, behold, I have brought the first of the fruits of the land which you, O Lord, have given me.** (Deuteronomy 26:5-10)

The farmer retells the whole story of the exodus. From Abraham's wandering, to Egypt, to slavery, to liberation. In first person plural. *My father. We cried out. God brought us out.*

And then he presents his fruits.

What I love about this ritual is what it refuses to do. It refuses to let the farmer act as if his harvest is purely his. As if he grew the fruit alone. As if the fruit exists in isolation from a long history.

Instead, the ritual inserts the story. The farmer can't offer his fruit without telling the whole backstory of how he got to be someone who has fruit to offer.

He's not from nowhere. He's the descendant of a wandering Aramean. His people were slaves. They were liberated by God. They came to this land. They worked the land. And now he has fruit.

The fruit in his basket is connected, through this retelling, to everything that preceded it. His prosperity is not a private achievement. It's the end of a long arc. And the ritual makes him name that arc, out loud, every year, as a condition of offering the first fruits.

This strikes me as deeply important. Our culture tends to narrate prosperity as individual achievement. *I worked hard. I built this. I earned this.* And to some extent, that's true. But it's only a small slice of the truth.

The fuller story includes the family that raised you. The society that educated you. The infrastructure that made your work possible. The strangers who, in a hundred small ways, helped you get to where you are. The history that shaped the possibilities available to you.

The farmer of Ki Tavo is required to remember all of that when he offers his first fruits. And not just to remember—to *say it out loud*. To tell the story.

There's something transformative in the saying out loud. It's one thing to know, abstractly, that you didn't get here alone. It's another to stand in a sacred place and narrate, in words, the whole chain of causation.

The narration changes your relationship to what you have. You hold it differently. Not as something owed to you. Not as something you deserve on your merits alone. But as something you've *received*, as the latest in a long sequence of gifts.

And this, maybe, is why the ritual of first fruits is placed here, in the final book of the Torah. The people are about to enter the land. They're about to become prosperous. They're about to build houses, plant vineyards, harvest grain. And the danger, Moses knows, is that they'll forget where they came from.

The annual ritual of first fruits is the antidote. Every year, at the beginning of the harvest, you stop. You go to the Temple. You tell the story. *My father was a wandering Aramean. We were slaves. God brought us out.* And now this fruit. Not because I'm great. Because the story is.

## What We Carry Forward

Whatever you have—in whatever domain you have it—is connected to a long chain of things you didn't do alone. This week, try telling the story out loud to yourself. Not the "I worked hard" version. The longer version. Who raised you. Who helped you. What history made your position possible. The fruit in your basket is part of a story. Name the whole story.
