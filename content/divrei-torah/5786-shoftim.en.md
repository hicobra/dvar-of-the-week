The parsha gives us one of the most quoted lines in all of Torah.

> **Justice, justice shall you pursue.** (Deuteronomy 16:20)

*Tzedek, tzedek tirdof.* Justice, justice. Two times. And then: pursue.

The rabbis, as they always do, ask about the repetition. Why *tzedek* twice? Why say it doubled?

Their answers are various and beautiful. One of the most famous is from the Kotzker Rebbe: pursue justice *justly*. Meaning, the means by which you seek justice must themselves be just. You can't get to a just end through unjust means. The path has to match the destination.

This is a more demanding teaching than it first seems. Because it's easy to convince ourselves that if the cause is righteous enough, the methods don't matter. We're fighting for something important. The ends justify the means. We can cut corners, break rules, deceive a little, cheat a little, as long as the outcome is just.

The Kotzker rejects this entirely. Justice, he says, is not only a result. It's a *process*. If you achieve a just outcome through manipulation, coercion, or dishonesty, you haven't actually achieved justice. You've achieved the appearance of justice while entrenching the habits of injustice.

The doubled word is insisting on both. The end has to be just. And the pursuit has to be just.

There's another reading I love. Some rabbis say the doubling is for emphasis. *Pursue justice urgently. Pursue it even when it's inconvenient. Pursue it even when you'd rather not.*

And the word *pursue*—*tirdof*—is striking. It's not "live justly" or "be just." It's *pursue*. Go after it. Chase it. Work for it. Justice doesn't happen by itself. It has to be actively sought.

The parsha places this verse in the context of setting up a legal system. Judges must be appointed. They must not take bribes. They must not show favoritism. They must judge the people fairly. And in the middle of all these institutional details, there's this poetic interjection: *justice, justice you shall pursue.*

It's as if Moses is saying: the structures matter, yes. The judges matter. The courts matter. But none of it works if the *pursuit of justice* isn't alive in the people. The institutions are scaffolding. Justice, as an active pursuit, is what the scaffolding is meant to hold up.

This is a deeply practical teaching. Systems matter. But they can be corrupted. Laws matter. But they can be twisted. What ultimately matters is whether justice is being actively pursued—by individuals, by communities, by those with power.

And the pursuit is *continuous*. You don't achieve justice once. You pursue it. The verb tense is ongoing.

This is why justice work is never finished. Every generation has to do it again. The forms of injustice change. New injustices emerge where old ones were solved. The pursuit doesn't have a finish line.

And in the meantime, the people pursuing justice have to be careful. Because power, even in the pursuit of justice, corrupts. You can become the thing you're fighting. The tools of injustice—deception, coercion, dehumanization—are always available, and they always promise faster results.

*Tzedek, tzedek*. Justice, justice. Both: the outcome and the method. Both: urgent and patient. Both: immediate and ongoing.

And then, *tirdof*. Pursue. Go after it. Don't wait for it to come to you.

## What We Carry Forward

You probably see an injustice somewhere in your life—in your workplace, your community, your family, the wider world. Pursuing justice doesn't mean solving it all at once. It means going after it. Taking one step toward it. And doing so *justly*. Not with the tools of the thing you're against. This week, take that step.
