Bnei Yisrael is trapped at the sea. Pharaoh's army is behind them. The water is in front. There's no way out. People are panicking. And Moshe, as you'd expect, is praying.

And God says something that stops me every time I read it.

> **וַיֹּאמֶר ה' אֶל־מֹשֶׁה מַה־תִּצְעַק אֵלָי דַּבֵּר אֶל־בְּנֵי־יִשְׂרָאֵל וְיִסָּעוּ** &middot; "And Hashem said to Moshe, 'Why are you crying out to Me? Speak to Bnei Yisrael and let them travel forward'" (Shemot 14:15).

*Why are you crying out to Me?*

This is a deeply strange verse. Moshe is doing what any good person would do in that moment. He's in crisis, so he's praying. And God pushes back. Not "keep praying." Not "I will save you." But: *why are you crying out? Tell them to move.*

The Or HaChaim picks up on this and says something worth sitting with. There's a kind of praying that's a substitute for action, and there's a kind of praying that's a preparation for action. God is not against prayer. God is against prayer that has become a way of staying put.

At the edge of the sea, the problem wasn't insufficient tefillah. The problem was that nobody was moving. Prayer, at that moment, was the wrong move — because it was the *only* move. The sea was not going to split because the prayers were heartfelt. The sea was going to split because someone stepped forward.

The midrash famously names that someone: Nachshon, who walked into the water up to his neck before it parted. The sea doesn't split for the prayer. The sea splits for the step.

This is important, I think, for how we sometimes relate to our own hard moments. It's easy to pray about something for weeks — really pray, sincerely pray — and mistake the prayer for the work. And sometimes the voice we don't want to hear whispers the same thing God says to Moshe: *why are you crying out? Speak to them. Travel forward.*

None of this is to say prayer is wrong. The rest of the Torah is full of prayer. It's to say that there's a difference between prayer that opens a door and prayer that keeps the door shut because at least you're doing something.

## Takeaway

There's something you've been praying about for a while. The question God asks Moshe at the sea is worth asking yourself: is the prayer the work, or is it a way of not doing the work? If it's the second, make the move. The sea split *after* they walked.
