"Be holy, for I am holy."

It's one of the most famous opening lines of Torah. And it's also one of the most confusing. Be holy. How? What does holiness look like? How do you become something that's presented as a characteristic of God—the ultimate authority—without any instruction on method?

The answer is in what comes next.

Instead of defining holiness in abstract terms, the parsha launches into concrete laws. Don't curse your parents. Keep the Sabbath. Don't turn to idols. Leave portions of your harvest for the poor. Don't steal. Don't bear false witness. Don't hate your brother in your heart. Love your neighbor as yourself.

These are not acts of holiness in the conventional sense. They're not mystical or transcendent. They're mundane. Some of them are about property. Some are about family conflict. Some are about keeping your promises.

But the parsha is saying: *This* is what holiness looks like. Not ecstasy. Not separation from the world. Not the achievement of some elevated spiritual state. Holiness is what you do Monday through Thursday. It's how you treat the person you're related to who annoys you. It's whether you charge interest on a loan.

The phrase "be holy" is stated as an imperative. You have to *do* it. Holiness is not something you have or achieve. It's something you *enact*, moment by moment, in relationship to other people and the world around you.

This is radically different from most spirituality. We usually imagine holiness as something you step into through effort and discipline. A state you reach. A level you attain. The parsha suggests it's something you *do*, habitually, in the way you treat people and property and time.

The parsha includes what we call the "Golden Rule"—love your neighbor as yourself—and places it in context with laws about how to do business, how to speak, how to resolve disputes. The love of neighbor is not separate from these practical laws; it *is* enacted through them.

If you love your neighbor as yourself, you won't charge them unjust interest. You won't hold a grudge. You won't gossip about them. These are the concrete expressions of the abstract principle.

And the beautiful thing is that this framework is achievable. You can't achieve ecstasy every day. You can't be spiritually elevated all the time. But you *can* avoid gossip. You *can* leave something in your field for the poor. You *can* keep your word. You *can* treat someone with the same care you'd want for yourself.

Holiness, the parsha suggests, is not above the everyday world. It's *in* the everyday world, in the way you move through it.

This also solves the problem of the opening line. You can't become like God in power or knowledge. You can't access the divine directly. But you *can* imitate God in the way you treat others. God is holy; God treats each person as valuable; therefore, be holy by treating each person as valuable. The imitation is not in metaphysical status but in behavior and orientation.

Most of the parsha is law. Dry, specific, practical. Not a single mystical or poetic image in it. Just: here's what holiness looks like. It looks like whether you speak truth. It looks like whether you help someone who fell. It looks like whether you charge fair prices.

The parsha trusts that if you live this way—concretely, ethically, moment by moment—you will have achieved something that deserves the name "holiness."

## What We Carry Forward

You probably thought holiness was something that required specialized practice or advanced spirituality. The parsha says: be holy this week by the way you speak to three people who annoy you. That's it. That's what holiness looks like from the inside.
