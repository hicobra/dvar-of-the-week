The book of Leviticus opens with a word that's easier to miss than it should be.

> **And the Lord called to Moses** (Leviticus 1:1)

*Vayikra.* He called. Not "the Lord said"—which is how most divine communication in Torah begins. Not "the Lord spoke." But *called*. A summoning. A summons.

The Midrash notices the difference. It sees in this particular verb a kind of intimacy that other verbs for speech don't quite capture. When God *calls* someone, it's different from when He *speaks* to them. A call requires that you be listening. It's directional. It assumes attention.

But here's what's strange: the calling happens before anything is actually said. God calls Moses—and then, presumably, Moses listens. And *then* comes the content of what God needs to communicate about sacrifices and offerings.

We're so used to having the message come first. Someone calls out, and we think: *there's a message coming*. We brace for information. But the Torah separates the call from the message. First: the summoning. Then: the listening. *Then*: the words.

Most of our communication works backward from this. We send the message—the email, the text, the announcement—and hope someone pays attention. We front-load the content and hope the attention follows. The medium is secondary. The message is primary.

But Leviticus suggests something different. Before any offering can be brought, before any law can be understood, someone has to *answer* a call. The receptivity has to come first. The willingness to be summoned, to interrupt what you're doing, to turn and listen—that comes before understanding is even possible.

This might be why Leviticus, of all the books of Torah, is the one that opens with a call rather than a command or an announcement. Leviticus is about the detailed architecture of sacrifice and service. It's dense. It's technical. It's going to require sustained attention. You can't halfheartedly read your way through Leviticus. You have to be called to it first.

The other books of Torah assume you're already listening. By the time we reach the laws and stories, the bargain is struck—the reader has bought in. But Leviticus can't assume that. It has to call first. *Then* ask you to listen to the details.

There's also something about call-and-response that matters here. When God calls Moses—not just once but *establishes* this as the pattern—Moses has to be ready to respond. Not passively listening, but actively answering. The Hebrew is *vayomer Moshe*: Moses answered. The call assumes the response. The communication isn't just one-way transmission; it's a relationship.

Maybe that's what we're missing. We send messages into the void and hope someone responds. We call numbers and leave voicemails. We post things publicly. Vayikra suggests that real communication requires someone on the other end who is willing to be called, who sees the summons as meant for them, and who answers back.

The sacrifice system that follows in Leviticus is, at its root, an answering to a call. A person brings an offering—which is their answer to God's summons. They're saying: *I heard you call me. I'm here. This is my response.*

## What We Carry Forward

You're being called, probably today, probably several times. Most of those calls you'll ignore or miss. But when you *do* recognize one—when something summons your real attention—notice whether you answer like you were waiting to be called, or like you're caught off guard. The difference matters more than the message.
