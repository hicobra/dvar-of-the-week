At the end of his life, Moses sings.

> **Give ear, O heavens, and I will speak; and hear, O earth, the words of my mouth.** (Deuteronomy 32:1)

The parsha is, almost entirely, a song. Moses's farewell song. The one God told him to teach the people as a witness against them when they go astray.

It's an unusual thing to end a life with. Most biographies don't end in verse. Most leaders don't give their final instructions in poetry. But Moses does.

Why a song?

Rashi has an answer that I think about a lot. He says: songs are remembered. They stay in your mouth. They go with you. Prose teachings can be forgotten. Songs are sticky.

Moses, knowing he's about to die, wants something to stay with the people. Not just information. Not just law. Something that will stay in their ears and their hearts across generations. Something they'll catch themselves humming.

So he gives them a song.

And it's not an easy song. It's not sentimental. It retells the story of their relationship with God—the rescue from Egypt, the provision in the wilderness, their turning to other gods, the consequences that followed. It's a song of love and betrayal and restoration.

It's meant to be a *witness*. Meaning: when the people go astray, this song will testify against them. It will call to mind what they were told. What they received. What they abandoned.

This is what songs do, in a deep way. They're not just entertainment. They're carriers of memory that work below the level of argument. You might forget a commandment. You're less likely to forget a song you learned as a child.

There's something about Moses choosing song as his final form. Song admits that prose isn't enough. That some truths need to be felt before they can be thought. That memory works through rhythm and repetition, not just through logic.

The song is long—forty-three verses—and it spans the whole arc of the people's experience. Past, present, future. Faithfulness, rebellion, punishment, restoration. It's compressed theology and history, set to a rhythm that can be sung.

And at the very end of the parsha, after the song, God tells Moses to go up the mountain. Mount Nebo. Where he will see the land. Where he will die.

Moses has one parsha left after this. But the essential teaching is here. The song is the summary. Everything Moses knows, compressed into a poem. Everything he's learned, given as a gift the people can carry.

There's also something about songs being communal. You can read alone. You usually sing together. A song given to a people is given *to the people*—meant to be shared, shared *in* being sung.

So when Moses gives Israel this song, he's giving them something they can hold together. A communal memory. A shared vocabulary. Something to return to, generation after generation.

The Hebrew word for song, *shir*, comes from a root that also means "circle." A song goes around. It comes back. It doesn't have a linear endpoint. You sing it, and then someone sings it again, and it keeps going.

That's what Moses gives the people before he dies. A song that will keep going. Not a commandment they might get around to observing. Not a teaching they might eventually understand. A song that will simply continue, sung by mouths that weren't even born yet when it was first given.

This is, in its way, a form of immortality. Not that Moses lives forever. But his song does. And his song carries what he needs the people to remember.

## What We Carry Forward

The things that stay with people, in the end, are often the ones given in song, in story, in ritual. Not the reasoned arguments. Not the written instructions. The things that get into the body. This week, think about what you want to leave with the people who matter to you. Could it be given as a song? As a story? In a form sticky enough to last?
