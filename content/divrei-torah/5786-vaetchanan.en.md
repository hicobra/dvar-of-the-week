In the middle of Vaetchanan sits the most famous prayer in Judaism.

> **Hear, O Israel: The Lord is our God, the Lord is One. You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your might.** (Deuteronomy 6:4-5)

The Shema. Said twice a day by observant Jews. Said by children as their first prayer and by the dying as their last.

What I want to sit with is that word: *all*. Not *some*. Not *enough*. *All*.

Love God with all your heart. With all your soul. With all your might.

The rabbis, who always pay attention to words that seem redundant, ask: why three? Why not just say "love God completely"? Why specify heart, soul, and might separately?

Their answer becomes a classic teaching. Each phrase, they say, refers to a different dimension of the self.

*With all your heart*—your inner life. Your thoughts. Your emotions. Your will.

*With all your soul*—your very life. Even to the point of death, if necessary. Martyrdom. Giving everything up, including existence itself.

*With all your might*—your resources. Your property. Your money. Your possessions.

And they notice something striking in this list. Heart and soul are, arguably, equally precious to everyone. But *might*—possessions, resources—varies enormously. Some people have very little. Some have a great deal. And for some people, the rabbis say, *might is even harder to give than life itself*.

Meaning: some people would die before they'd give away their money.

This is a dark observation, but it's also honest. For many of us, what we *own* is more deeply attached to us than our theoretical willingness to die for something. We'll say we'd die for a cause. But ask us to give up our apartment, our savings, our property—and we get stingy very fast.

The Shema is asking for all three. Inner life. Life itself. Resources. Everything we are and everything we have.

This is a huge demand. It's also, in its own way, the most liberating demand possible. Because the opposite of "love God with all" isn't "love God with some." The opposite is "love yourself with all"—making yourself the center of everything, and treating everything else as instrumental to you.

The Shema is redirecting the center. Not my heart, my soul, my might—but *God's* heart, soul, might, as received by me and returned to God.

I don't think this is easy or that any of us does it perfectly. But it's a direction. It's a way of orienting.

And notice what the verse *doesn't* say. It doesn't say "love God with your theological understanding." It doesn't say "love God with your ritual observance." It doesn't even say "love God with your faith." It says: *love God with all you have*.

This is a love that's total, concrete, and embodied. It includes your feelings, yes. But also your actual life. Your actual possessions. Your actual daily choices about time and energy and money.

The Shema says these are all the currency of love. Not just the poetic ones.

There's a reason, I think, that this prayer has stayed at the center of Jewish practice for three thousand years. It's simple. It's brief. And it's honest about what love costs.

## What We Carry Forward

Love, in this prayer, isn't just a feeling. It's your heart, yes—but also your life and your possessions. This week, notice where your resources are actually going. What are you actually loving with your time and money? If it's not aligned with what you say you value, the prayer suggests the mismatch is worth addressing.
