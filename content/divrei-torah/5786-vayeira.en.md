Avraham is three days into recovery from his brit milah. He's ninety-nine years old. It's the hottest part of the day. He's sitting at the entrance of his tent, probably not even well enough to be standing up. And then three strangers appear in the distance.

> **וַיַּרְא וַיָּרָץ לִקְרָאתָם מִפֶּתַח הָאֹהֶל** &middot; *Vayar vayaratz likratam mipetach ha'ohel* &middot; "He saw them and he ran toward them from the entrance of the tent" (Bereishit 18:2).

The word that stops me in that verse is *vayaratz* — and he ran. He ran. At ninety-nine. Three days after circumcising himself.

The Torah doesn't usually slow down to tell us about gait. It could have said "he went," "he approached," "he stood up." It says he *ran*.

There's something in this about what hachnasat orchim actually is. It's easy to think hospitality is doing the thing — the meal, the bed, the welcoming. Avraham's not just doing it. He's running at it.

And that's a very different picture than what most of us have in mind. We tend to think of hospitality as an obligation to meet — guests arrive, we do what's expected. The Torah is showing us something else. The mitzvah isn't what you serve. It's the speed you go toward the door.

You can tell a lot about what someone actually cares about by watching how fast they move toward it. Nobody runs toward something they're indifferent about. Avraham's run is the tell. He isn't putting up with guests. He's excited to have them.

And here's the part that's easy to miss: he doesn't know who they are. From the outside, they're three strangers in the desert. He's not running because they're important. He's running because they showed up, and they're hungry and thirsty, and that's enough.

I think about this whenever I have people over. The meal matters. The conversation matters. But the piece the Torah is highlighting is the three seconds when they're at the door — before they've even come in — and you either stand up and go to them, or you stay where you are and wait for them to come to you.

It's a small moment. But the Torah gives it a whole verb.

## Takeaway

Hospitality isn't what's on the table. It's the speed you move toward the door. When someone comes over this week, go to meet them before they reach you. That's the whole thing.
