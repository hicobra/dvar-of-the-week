The plagues are winding down. Pharaoh is about to let the people go. Bnei Yisrael is on the edge of freedom. And right here, before the Exodus even happens, God gives Moshe the first mitzvah that will belong to Bnei Yisrael as a people.

It's not about faith. It's not about prayer. It's not about remembering. It's about the calendar.

> **הַחֹדֶשׁ הַזֶּה לָכֶם רֹאשׁ חֳדָשִׁים רִאשׁוֹן הוּא לָכֶם לְחָדְשֵׁי הַשָּׁנָה** &middot; "This month shall be for you the head of months, the first of the months of the year" (Shemot 12:2).

The very first commandment to Bnei Yisrael is: here is your calendar. Nisan is your first month. *You* are in charge of marking the months now.

Take a second to appreciate how strange this is. Of all the possible first mitzvot — "believe in Me," "pray to Me," "remember My wonders" — the Torah picks this. The calendar. The months.

The Ibn Ezra and others explain why. A slave does not own time. A slave's day is given to him. His mornings, his evenings, his weeks — they all belong to someone else. A free person marks time themselves. They decide when the month begins. They decide what the year is doing.

And so the first gift of freedom is not a piece of land. It's not a set of rules. It's not a promise. It's a calendar.

There's something very modern in this too. A lot of what makes a person feel unfree in their own life — even long after any actual slavery is over — is that their time belongs to something else. Work dictates the week. Obligations dictate the day. Decades can go by without you feeling like you *chose* the shape of your year.

The Torah opens the mitzvot with a correction to that. Before anything else — own the calendar. Decide what this month is for. Decide what this Shabbat will hold. Decide when the year turns over. Because if you don't, the calendar will be set for you, and you will live inside someone else's time again.

## Takeaway

The first mitzvah ever given to us was: *you* decide what the month is. Before this week ends, try it once. Look at your calendar, and put something there that's yours — not something someone else put. That's what the Exodus opens with.
