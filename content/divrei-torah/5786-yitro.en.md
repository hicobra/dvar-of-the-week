The Parsha with the Ten Commandments — the biggest moment in the whole Torah — is named after a Midianite priest who wasn't even there for most of it.

> **וַיִּשְׁמַע יִתְרוֹ כֹהֵן מִדְיָן חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה אֵת כָּל־אֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה אֱלֹהִים לְמֹשֶׁה וּלְיִשְׂרָאֵל עַמּוֹ** &middot; "And Yitro, the priest of Midian, Moshe's father-in-law, heard all that God had done for Moshe and for Yisrael His people" (Shemot 18:1).

Stop for a second on the name. The parsha is called *Yitro*. Not *Aseret HaDibrot* (The Ten Commandments). Not *Matan Torah*. Not *Sinai*. *Yitro*. After a man who isn't even Jewish.

And why? Because right before the revelation at Sinai, Yitro shows up and gives Moshe a piece of very practical advice: you can't judge everyone yourself. You'll burn out. Set up a system. Appoint judges. Delegate.

It's boring, organizational, managerial advice. From an outsider. And Moshe listens. And the Torah preserves the moment with such weight that the whole parsha — the one where God Himself speaks to an entire nation — is named after the father-in-law who told Moshe to organize his week better.

There's something very deep in this.

The Torah is making a quiet statement about where wisdom comes from. Yitro is not part of the covenant. He's not a prophet. He's a priest of a foreign religion. And yet the Torah says, with no apology: he was right, and Moshe was wise enough to know it.

The Maharal on this parsha notes that humility about one's sources is itself a Torah value. A person who only accepts wisdom from inside his own circle ends up narrower than the truth. A person who can hear a good idea from anywhere — even from someone whose whole framework seems different from his own — is living more fully inside the Torah than someone who is technically observant but can't hear a wise word unless it wears the right clothing.

The Torah was about to be given. The greatest moment in history was about to happen. And the parsha is named after the reminder: the wise thing someone just said to you — listen to it, even if it came from somewhere unexpected.

## Takeaway

Someone in your life this week is going to say something useful that you didn't expect. Not from the usual source. Maybe not in the way you wanted to hear it. The parsha that contains the Ten Commandments is named after someone like that. Listen to them.
