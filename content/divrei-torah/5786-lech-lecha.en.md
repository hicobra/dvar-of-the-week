The two famous words at the start of the Parsha — *lech lecha* — are actually a little weird if you stop to look at them. God says to Avraham, "Go, go." Two forms of the same verb, one right after the other.

> **לֶךְ־לְךָ מֵאַרְצְךָ וּמִמּוֹלַדְתְּךָ וּמִבֵּית אָבִיךָ אֶל־הָאָרֶץ אֲשֶׁר אַרְאֶךָּ** &middot; "Go for yourself, from your land and your birthplace and your father's house, to the land that I will show you" (Bereishit 12:1).

Rashi notices the doubled word and gives it a famous reading: *lech lecha* means "go for yourself" — or, the way it often gets said, "go to yourself." God isn't just telling Avraham to go somewhere. God is telling him to go become someone.

The land is kind of the excuse. The person he's going to be is the real point.

Look at what he's told to leave. Three things, in this order: your land, your birthplace, your father's house. Wide, narrower, narrowest. Your country, sure. Your hometown, okay. But your father's house? That's the room you grew up in. The voice in your head about what you should do with your life. The habits you don't even know you have. And God is saying — leave that too.

Not abandon. Leave. Avraham isn't disowning his past. He's just walking out from under it.

And then comes the part that I think is the hardest. God says, "to the land that I will show you." That's it. No name. No map. Just — start walking, and you'll see.

That's how it usually works, actually. We want to know the whole plan before we take the first step. But that's not how it goes here. You go, and then you find out where you're going. The clarity shows up after you start, not before.

Avraham is seventy-five when this happens. That's in the Torah on purpose. It's never too late to hear *lech lecha*. And the trip was never really about the place.

## Takeaway

The hardest thing to leave isn't the country. It's the house of habits you grew up inside. You don't need the whole map. You need the first step. The land will show up.
