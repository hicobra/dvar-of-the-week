The parsha opens with one word in the title that's worth stopping on.

> **וַיַּקְהֵל מֹשֶׁה אֶת־כָּל־עֲדַת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל** &middot; *Vayakhel Moshe et kol adat Bnei Yisrael* &middot; "And Moshe gathered the entire community of Bnei Yisrael" (Shemot 35:1).

*Vayakhel*. He gathered. Not a word you'd bother with — until you remember what came right before.

Right before Vayakhel is Ki Tisa. The golden calf. The worst communal failure in the whole Torah. A people who, not long after standing at Sinai together, had fractured so badly that three thousand of them were killed by the Levi'im in the aftermath. The community was broken. Not metaphorically — literally. People who had stood side by side at the mountain were now on opposite sides of a civil wound.

And the very next parsha, the very first thing Moshe does, is *gather them*.

Not teach them. Not rebuke them. Not rebuild the Mishkan. Not even start new. He *gathers them*.

The Sfas Emes picks up on this ordering and says something beautiful. The Mishkan in this parsha is ultimately about God dwelling among us. But you cannot house a Divine presence in a community that's broken in half. Before the gathering of the materials for the sanctuary, there has to be a gathering of the people. The Mishkan is going to be built from their contributions — but only after they're an "us" again.

This is a deeply practical Torah. It's telling us what the first step is when something has fractured. Not explanation. Not apology. Not even rebuilding, yet. First — *gathering*. Getting the people back in the same room.

There's something restorative about the simple act of being gathered. Not gathered *around* an agreement, or *around* a shared vision — just, back in a space together. Before the conversation can happen, before the repair can happen, before anything new can be built, you have to be in the same room.

After a fracture in a family, a community, a marriage, a friendship — the first move is very rarely the right words. It's usually the room. Sitting back down in the same room. The talking comes after the gathering.

## Takeaway

If there's something fractured in your life right now, don't lead with the fix. Lead with the gathering. Moshe didn't rebuild the broken people first. He gathered them. Everything else followed.
