Twelve men. Forty days. One land. Two reports.

Moses sends twelve leaders—one from each tribe—to scout the land of Israel. They walk through it for forty days. They see the same hills. They eat the same fruit. They meet the same peoples. They see the same cities.

And then they come back and tell two completely different stories.

Ten of them say: the land is amazing. We saw giants. We saw fortified cities. We can't do this. We looked like grasshoppers in their eyes. And they were right to look at us that way—we are grasshoppers.

Two of them—Joshua and Caleb—say: the land is amazing. We can do this. God is with us. Let's go.

What's striking is that both sides agree on the data. Everyone saw giants. Everyone saw the fortified cities. Everyone noticed the land was rich. The *facts* are not in dispute.

What differs is how they hold the facts. And what they say about themselves.

The ten spies say: "We were like grasshoppers in our own eyes, and so we were in their eyes." Notice the structure. *We were like grasshoppers in our own eyes*—first. *And so we were in their eyes*—second. The perception of themselves came before the perception by others.

The rabbis notice this. They say: the spies' fundamental failure wasn't that they saw giants. It was that they saw themselves as grasshoppers. The giants didn't diminish them. They diminished themselves, and then projected that diminishment onto how the giants saw them.

Caleb and Joshua looked at the exact same situation and didn't do that. They didn't say "they're giants and we're small." They said "God is with us." Their self-image wasn't based on a comparison with the inhabitants of the land. It was based on a different frame entirely.

This is, I think, one of the most practical Torah teachings anywhere. The question isn't whether there are giants. There are giants. There are big, scary, fortified things in every direction. The question is how you see yourself in relation to them.

Do you see yourself as a grasshopper facing giants? Or do you see yourself as someone walking with God through a land that's been given to you?

The ten spies weren't lying. They weren't exaggerating. They were accurately describing what they saw. But accuracy isn't the same as truth. The frame you put around facts matters as much as the facts themselves.

And here's what's tragic: the generation that said "we can't do this" didn't get to go in. They wandered for forty years until they died out. Caleb and Joshua—the two who said "we can"—those two, with a new generation, eventually entered the land.

It's not that God punished the ten spies for their report. It's that their report *was already the punishment*. If you see yourself as a grasshopper, you can't enter a land of giants. Not because God won't let you—but because grasshoppers don't enter lands.

The ten spies exiled themselves through their self-perception before any divine decree was made. The decree just made it formal.

This is the brutal and freeing truth: the frame you put around your life becomes your life. If you see yourself as small, your life becomes small. If you see yourself as walking with God, your life becomes that walk.

## What We Carry Forward

There are giants in your life. Real ones. Big, scary, fortified things. The question isn't whether they're there—they are. The question is what you see when you look at yourself standing in front of them. This week, try to see what Caleb saw. Not a grasshopper. Just yourself, walking with God.
