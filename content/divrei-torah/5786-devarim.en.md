The fifth book of the Torah opens with the simplest possible title.

> **These are the words that Moses spoke to all Israel.** (Deuteronomy 1:1)

*Eileh ha-devarim.* These are the words. And the whole book that follows is mostly Moses speaking—a long farewell address that takes up almost the whole last month of his life.

Moses is going to die. He knows it. God has told him he won't enter the land. So he does something that I think is deeply human: he uses the time he has to *speak*.

He doesn't leave instructions. He doesn't hand over a written document. He stands in front of the people and he talks. He retells their story. He reminds them of where they've been. He warns them about where they're going. He pleads with them, teaches them, sings to them.

Forty years of wilderness experience, compressed into one sustained speech.

What's striking is how much of the book is review. Moses recounts the exodus. He recounts the sin of the golden calf. He recounts the sending of the spies. He recounts the arguments, the rebellions, the miracles. Almost nothing in Deuteronomy is new. It's all a retelling.

The rabbis call this book *Mishneh Torah*—"the repetition of the Torah." That's where the English name comes from: Deuteronomy, "second law."

But repetition here isn't redundancy. Moses is doing something specific. He's *integrating* the experience of the people. Telling them who they are by telling them where they've been. Making sure the memory of it goes with them into the land.

Because here's the thing: Moses won't be there. The people are going into the land without him. And once they're in the land, with its farms and its wars and its seasons, they'll be tempted to forget. To think they're a normal nation with a normal history. To think the wilderness, the exodus, the mountain—all that was someone else's story.

So Moses speaks. He tells them the story one more time, in their own hearing, to make sure it stays in them.

There's a lesson in this about endings. Moses doesn't spend his last month doing more. He doesn't try to squeeze in more action, more miracles, more legislation. He spends it *speaking*. He gives the people his words.

This is what you give someone, in the end. Not things. Not accomplishments. Words. What you've understood. What you've come to see. What you want them to remember.

The book of Deuteronomy is Moses giving his words to the next generation. Not his possessions—he doesn't have many. Not his authority—that passes to Joshua. His *words*. What he has learned. What he needs them to know.

And the Torah preserves them, all of them, so that three thousand years later, we're still listening to this speech.

There's a kind of grace in being able to say what you've come to know before you go. Not everyone gets this. Many people die suddenly, mid-sentence. But Moses is given time to speak. And he uses it.

## What We Carry Forward

If you had thirty days to say what you've come to know, to the people who matter to you, what would you say? The book of Deuteronomy suggests that question isn't morbid. It's clarifying. You don't have forever. Start saying the things that need to be said.
