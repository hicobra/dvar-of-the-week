The word *Tzav* means "command." It's direct. Unambiguous. When God says *tzav*, He's not suggesting or implying. He's commanding. And the parsha that bears this name is one command after another—detailed instructions about how the priest is to manage the offerings.

But there's something odd about the placement of this parsha. Everything in it is a repetition of material already given in Parshat Vayikra, just one week ago. The same offerings. The same laws. The same procedures. Why repeat them?

The answer lies in what's changed between the two parshiot. In Vayikra, the laws are given as a general system—here's how offering *works*. In Tzav, the laws are given again, but this time as orders to the *priest*. Here's what *you* do.

It's the difference between learning a system and receiving a job.

When you first encounter a complicated process—how to run a department, how to manage a project, how to lead a team—you often learn the broad strokes. Here's the system. Here's how it works. Here's the logic behind it. But that's not the same as understanding what *you're* supposed to do *in* that system.

The transition from Vayikra to Tzav is the transition from theory to responsibility.

And it's specifically the priest who bears that responsibility. The priest doesn't just *understand* the offering system. The priest *does* the offering system. Every day. For everyone else. It becomes his job—not his knowledge, but his obligation.

This is harder than just knowing something. Any of the Israelites could potentially understand the offerings. But only the priest is bound to execute them, over and over, even when no one is watching, even when the procedures seem rote, even when the people bringing the offerings don't quite understand what they're doing.

We live in a culture obsessed with understanding. We want to know *why*. We want to understand the system before we commit to it. We read the manual before we start. We want the full explanation.

But responsibility often arrives before full understanding does. You become a parent before you know how to parent. You take a job before you fully understand the role. You join a community before you've internalized all its unwritten rules. And in those moments, you don't get a luxury of a month-long orientation. You get a command: *Now you do this.*

The priest couldn't wait until the system made complete sense to him. The offerings had to happen. So the command came: Here's what you do, day after day, whether or not you can see the purpose in every detail.

There's something purifying about that, actually. The priest's job is to do the work regardless of whether it *feels* meaningful on any given day. Whether he's moved, whether he understands the theology, whether the person bringing the offering is sincere—the work happens. The procedure is done. The system is maintained.

For most of us, that sounds limiting. We want meaning to precede action. But tzav—the commandment—inverts that. The action comes first. The meaning often follows.

## What We Carry Forward

There's something in your life that you understand better by doing than by thinking about. It's likely something someone asked you to do before you felt ready. Do it today the way a priest would do the morning offering—with care, on schedule, whether or not you feel inspired. The meaning will come.
