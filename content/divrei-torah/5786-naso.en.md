In the middle of Naso, the longest parsha in the Torah, sits a short, surprising passage. Fifteen Hebrew words. The priestly blessing.

> **May the Lord bless you and keep you. May the Lord shine His face upon you and be gracious to you. May the Lord lift His face toward you and give you peace.** (Numbers 6:24-26)

These fifteen words have been said for over three thousand years. By priests in the Temple. By parents over their children on Friday nights. By rabbis at weddings. By anyone who wanted to bless someone and didn't have their own words.

What's striking about them is what they don't say. They don't specify what the blessing is *for*. There's no "may you have children" or "may you prosper in business" or "may your enemies be defeated." There's no *content* to the blessing. Just: may God bless you. May He keep you. May His face shine on you. May you have peace.

The blessing is abstract to the point of being almost empty. And that's the point.

The Sforno says: the specific blessings of life are given by God. The priest doesn't determine what you'll be blessed with. The priest just *opens the channel*. Creates the possibility. Invites the flow.

You might think blessings need to be specific to be effective. "I hope you get the job." "I hope you find love." But the priestly blessing suggests otherwise. It says: the specific things matter less than the general orientation. What matters is that you are turned toward blessing. That the face of God is turned toward you. That there is peace in your space.

The specific contents—the job, the love, the health—those come through blessing. But they come through because the channel is open. Not because you asked for them by name.

There's also something about the words *God's face*. The blessing asks twice that God's face be turned toward you—shining, lifted up. Not His hands, which would suggest action on your behalf. Not His voice, which would suggest instruction. His *face*. Meaning: His attention. His regard. His presence.

The deepest blessing, the parsha suggests, isn't being given something. It's being *seen*. It's being held in the attention of something that cares about you.

This is strangely tender for a blessing given by priests in a formal religious context. It's not ceremonial. It's intimate. It's the language of love, really—wanting to be looked at with care.

And the words end with *shalom*. Peace. Not just the absence of conflict, but wholeness. The condition of being intact, complete, unbroken. The blessing moves from God's presence toward you, and then it wants for you the thing that that presence makes possible: to be whole.

One more thing: the priests didn't invent these words. They didn't write them. They only said them. The blessing is given to them to speak, but they're just the channel. The blessing itself comes from God.

So the priest standing in front of the people, hands lifted, saying these ancient words, isn't personally blessing anyone. He's transmitting something larger. He's just the vessel through which the blessing passes.

We all have this capacity, I think. When we bless someone—whether in words, in gestures, in attention—we're not creating the blessing. We're transmitting something that's larger than us. The best thing we can do is stay out of its way.

## What We Carry Forward

Someone in your life needs to be blessed. Not with specific outcomes, but with your full, turned-toward attention. Try saying these fifteen words over them this week—aloud, if you can, or silently. You don't have to be a priest. You just have to be the channel.
