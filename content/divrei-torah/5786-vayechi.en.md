Yaakov dies at the end of this parsha. So does Yosef. It's the last parsha in Bereishit, the closing of an era.

And it's called *Vayechi* — "And he lived."

> **וַיְחִי יַעֲקֹב בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם שְׁבַע עֶשְׂרֵה שָׁנָה** &middot; *Vayechi Yaakov b'eretz Mitzrayim shva esreh shanah* &middot; "And Yaakov lived in the land of Egypt seventeen years" (Bereishit 47:28).

It's a strange title. The parsha is mostly about Yaakov's last days. His blessings. His funeral. His death. If you were naming a parsha by its contents, you'd probably call it "Vayamot" — "and he died." Or "The Blessings." Or "Burial." The word *vayechi* seems almost in the wrong place.

The Baal HaTurim picks up on this and notices the number: seventeen. Yaakov lives in Egypt for seventeen years. And those seventeen years — the Baal HaTurim says — were the best years of his life.

Think about that. Yaakov is 147 years old when he dies. He spent most of those years running a complicated life — wrestling with his brother, being deceived by his father-in-law, losing Yosef, watching his family fracture. And then he gets down to Egypt, at 130, an old man who had every reason to feel like his life was essentially over. And the next seventeen years turn out to be the chapter the Torah titles "and he lived."

There's something in this that pushes back against how we usually think about time. We tend to picture life as an arc — things rise, things peak, things decline. The last chapter is the sad one. The last chapter is where things are winding down.

And the Torah just quietly inverts that. No — the last seventeen years were the ones the Torah called "life." The earlier years were preparation. The end was where the actual living happened.

We don't get to choose our arc. But we do get to recognize, I think, that the end is not a denouement. It's not a slowing-down scene. It has the potential to be the chapter that earns the word *vayechi*. For Yaakov, it was.

That's worth sitting with. Especially on a parsha that closes the entire book of Bereishit — which means it's not just about Yaakov. It's a note the Torah leaves us at the end of the story of the patriarchs: the closing chapter is also life.

## Takeaway

The best years aren't always the loud ones. Yaakov's final seventeen were the ones the Torah called "life." Whatever chapter you're in — it may be the one that earns the word.
