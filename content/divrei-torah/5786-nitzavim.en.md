The parsha begins with a powerful image.

> **You are standing today, all of you, before the Lord your God—your tribal leaders, your elders, your officers, every man of Israel, your children, your wives, and the stranger who is in the midst of your camp, from the woodcutter to the water-drawer.** (Deuteronomy 29:9-10)

*Nitzavim.* Standing. Before God. Today.

Moses is about to die. The covenant is being renewed. Everyone is gathered—not just the important ones. Leaders and children. Husbands and wives. Citizens and strangers. The woodcutter. The water-drawer. The most marginal figures in the community are named explicitly.

*All of you.* Present. Standing. Today.

The midrash picks up on the word *today* and makes a beautiful move. It says: *today* doesn't just mean the specific historical day. It means today, whenever you read this. It means the standing is happening right now, whoever you are, whenever you encounter these words.

The covenant isn't a one-time event. It's an ongoing standing. Every Jew, the midrash says, stood at Sinai. And every Jew, every day, stands again.

This is a remarkable claim. It's saying that the covenant isn't historical, really. Or rather, it's historical *and* contemporary. The people who stood before Moses that day are standing with us now. And we're standing with them.

Moses goes on to say something strange, which is often quoted:

> **For this commandment which I command you today is not too hard for you, nor is it far off. It is not in heaven, that you should say, 'Who will go up to heaven for us and bring it down to us?' Nor is it beyond the sea, that you should say, 'Who will cross the sea for us and bring it to us?' But the word is very near to you, in your mouth and in your heart, that you may do it.** (Deuteronomy 30:11-14)

The Torah is not far away. It's not up in heaven. It's not across the sea. It's right here. In your mouth. In your heart. You can do it.

This is meant to be reassuring, and it is. But it's also challenging. Because it removes the easy excuses.

We'd like the Torah to be hard to reach. Up in heaven. Across the sea. Accessible only to scholars, only to mystics, only to the very pious. That way, we don't have to do it ourselves.

But Moses says: no. It's right here. In your own mouth. In your own heart. You don't need a specialist. You don't need an intermediary. You don't need perfect understanding. You just need to *do* what's in front of you.

The word for commandment, *mitzvah*, means something different than "commandment" does in English. In Hebrew, *mitzvah* is closer to "connection." The commandments are ways of connecting—to God, to community, to the past, to the moral order.

And Moses is saying: these connections are available to you. Right now. In your ordinary life. You can do them.

This is the final move of Deuteronomy, really. Moses has given his speeches. He has retold the story. He has laid out the laws. He has warned and blessed. And now he's saying: *you can do this. It's not beyond you. It's in you.*

And the posture is *standing*. Not sitting. Not kneeling. Standing. Upright. Present. On your feet. Ready.

There's agency in standing. There's responsibility. A person who is standing is a person who can act.

The whole community is in that posture. Leaders and marginals together. And all of them—all of us—are being told that the covenant is *in* us. Not somewhere else.

## What We Carry Forward

The thing you think you need to do requires less than you think. It's not up in heaven. It's not across the sea. It's in your mouth. It's in your heart. This week, stop looking for the expert, the guru, the distant authority. Stand. The covenant is in you.
