Yosef has had a hard run. Sold by his brothers. Bought by Potiphar. Framed by Potiphar's wife. Thrown into the king's prison for a crime he didn't commit. This is probably the lowest point in his life.

And it's from there that the Torah tells us something small but important.

> **וַיַּרְא אֹתָם וְהִנָּם זֹעֲפִים. וַיִּשְׁאַל אֶת־סְרִיסֵי פַרְעֹה ... לֵאמֹר מַדּוּעַ פְּנֵיכֶם רָעִים הַיּוֹם** &middot; "He saw them, and they were troubled. And he asked Pharaoh's servants... 'Why do your faces look bad today?'" (Bereishit 40:6–7).

Yosef is in prison. He has every excuse in the world to be curled up inside his own suffering. Two new prisoners arrive — the butler and the baker. They look depressed.

And Yosef *notices*.

He could have minded his own business. He could have not had the emotional bandwidth. What happens next is going to be the thing that eventually gets him out of prison — he hears their dreams, interprets them, and that chain of events leads to Pharaoh. But none of it starts because Yosef was ambitious. It starts because he noticed two sad faces on a bad day in a prison cell.

This is so easy to miss. We tend to tell the Yosef story as a drama of talent — the dream-interpreter whose gift lifts him from the pit to the palace. But the Torah makes sure we see the actual ignition point, and it's not talent. It's attention.

Specifically: attention in the wrong direction.

When we're struggling, our attention naturally moves inward. This is not a flaw — it's how humans work. We protect ourselves by narrowing our focus. But there's a Yosef move available to us, which is to look outward, even then. Especially then.

"Why do your faces look bad today?" is such a small question. It's a nothing question, really. And it's the question that quietly starts a whole redemption.

The Torah seems to be saying that the way out of your own pit sometimes begins with noticing somebody else's. Not as a strategy. Not as a lesson you're trying to learn. Just as a quiet, human reflex: I'm suffering, and I see that you are too, and I'm going to ask.

## Takeaway

This week, when you're carrying something hard — try the Yosef move. Look up. Notice the person next to you. Ask.
