There's a moment in the Balaam story that's easy to laugh at and easy to miss.

Balak, the king of Moab, has hired Balaam to curse Israel. Balaam saddles his donkey and rides off to do the cursing. And on the way, the donkey stops.

An angel with a drawn sword is standing in the path. The donkey sees the angel. Balaam, the prophet—the one hired to speak with the divine—does not see the angel.

The donkey steps aside. Balaam hits her. She stops again. He hits her harder. She lies down. He beats her.

And then God opens the donkey's mouth, and she speaks.

> **What have I done to you, that you have struck me these three times?** (Numbers 22:28)

The donkey is reasoning with the prophet. She's defending her behavior. She's more perceptive than he is. She can see what he can't.

This story is strange on many levels, but one level I keep coming back to is this: Balaam is the professional seer. His whole reputation is built on perceiving what others don't. And yet, in this moment, a donkey sees more than he does.

The Torah is making a point, I think. Perception is not about your title or your reputation or even your training. Perception is about whether your eyes happen to be open in that moment to what's actually there.

Balaam *wants* to see what he wants to see. He's been hired for a specific outcome. He's headed toward a specific curse. His sight is locked into the mission. He cannot see the angel because he's not looking for an angel.

The donkey has no agenda. She's just a donkey walking down a road. She doesn't want anything except to get where she's going without being hit. Her eyes are unclouded.

And because her eyes are unclouded, she sees.

This is a hard teaching because it suggests that expertise can blind us. The more invested we are in a particular outcome, the more we become like Balaam—unable to see what's actually in the path. Our training, our job, our self-image, our commitments—all of these can obscure what's right in front of us.

The donkey speaks for a few lines, and then the Torah tells us: *And the Lord opened Balaam's eyes.* Only then does he see the angel. Only then does he realize what he's been missing.

So even Balaam is capable of seeing. But he needed to be humbled first. He needed a donkey to outperceive him. He needed his own blindness to be made undeniable before his eyes could open.

Most of us don't have this kind of dramatic moment. We don't have donkeys speaking to us. What we have, usually, are quieter signs. A child's question that stops us. A friend's comment we didn't want to hear. A bodily sensation we've been ignoring. A pattern we keep refusing to see.

These are the moments when something that lacks our training, our status, our expertise—something small and humble and unassuming—sees what we can't. And the question is whether we hit it and keep going, or whether we stop and pay attention.

There's also this: the angel in the story is not there to stop Balaam from traveling. He's there to make sure Balaam doesn't curse Israel without knowing what he's doing. The angel is, in a sense, trying to save Balaam from himself. But Balaam can't receive the help because he can't see the help standing right in front of him.

The most dangerous blindness isn't to what's far from us. It's to what's nearest. The angel was in the path. The truth is in the path. It just takes a donkey, sometimes, to make you notice.

## What We Carry Forward

Something small and humble in your life is seeing what you can't. Your body. A child. A friend whose opinion you've been dismissing. This week, pay attention to what wants to stop you. Don't hit it. Look at what it's looking at.
