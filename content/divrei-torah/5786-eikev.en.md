The parsha opens with a strange word.

> **And it will be, *eikev*, because you listen to these judgments...** (Deuteronomy 7:12)

*Eikev.* Which could be translated "because." Or "as a result." But its literal meaning is *heel*. From the same root as Yaakov—Jacob, who was named because he was born grasping his brother's heel.

*Because*, in this verse, is a foot metaphor. Because you listen. On the heels of your listening. Right behind your steps.

Rashi picks up on this. He says the word *eikev* here refers to the *small* commandments—the ones people step on with their heels. The commandments that are easy to overlook. The ones that feel insignificant.

Eikev, in this reading, is warning us: pay attention to the small ones. The ones you're tempted to trample. Because the blessing comes *on the heels of those*, specifically. Not the big dramatic commandments. The small daily ones that you walk over without noticing.

This is a deeply humbling reading.

We tend to think spiritual life is measured by the big moments. The dramatic acts of faith. The major sacrifices. The times we really showed up.

But Eikev suggests that the actual texture of a spiritual life is in the small things. The commandments we step on without thinking. The quiet acts of decency that don't make headlines. The choices that happen at the heel of the foot, in the parts of life we consider mundane.

A person who keeps the big commandments but tramples the small ones is not, according to Eikev, doing well. The small ones matter. Maybe they matter more. Because they reveal what you're actually like when you think no one is watching—including yourself.

The parsha continues with a number of other important passages. The warning not to forget God when you become prosperous. The reminder that it's God who gives you the strength to make wealth. The account of the manna teaching us that we don't live by bread alone, but by what proceeds from God's mouth.

But the opening word frames all of it. *Eikev*. On the heels. The blessings come on the heels of something specific—your attention to what you're tempted to ignore.

This is, I think, how character actually forms. Not through dramatic moments of virtue. Through the accumulated weight of small, unnoticed choices. How you speak to someone in a service job. Whether you pick up a piece of trash you didn't drop. Whether you return a text you could easily ignore. Whether you give credit where you could quietly not.

These are the heel-commandments. The ones where the choice is small enough that you could easily go either way, and probably no one would know the difference. And Eikev says: *those are exactly where blessing is rooted*.

There's a connection here to the name Jacob. Jacob was born grasping Esau's heel—holding onto his brother, not letting go. And Jacob, in the Torah's long arc, becomes Israel—the one who wrestles with God. The transformation from Jacob to Israel happens through a lifetime of attention to the parts of life others would overlook. Working fourteen years for the wife he loved. Returning to face the brother he had wronged. Refusing to let go of the divine encounter at the Jabbok until he received a blessing.

Jacob's whole story is about the heel—about grasping, about not letting go of what's small and important. And Israel, the name he earns, is what comes after that long faithfulness.

## What We Carry Forward

The small things you step on every day are probably not small. Someone you barely acknowledged. A choice you made on autopilot. A moment of honesty you could have offered and didn't. This week, try to walk on your heels for once. Pay attention to what you'd normally tread on. The blessing is there.
