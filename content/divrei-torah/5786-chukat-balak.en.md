When Chukat and Balak are read together, a shared theme emerges that neither parsha makes fully explicit on its own: the limits of sight.

Chukat gives us the red heifer—the law whose logic is beyond human reason. The ashes that purify the impure and defile the pure. The rabbis call it *chok*—a decree we're instructed to follow without comprehending. Solomon, the wisest of men, confessed he couldn't penetrate its logic.

Balak gives us Balaam—the professional prophet who can't see the angel standing in his path. A donkey sees it. He doesn't. His vision is clouded by his assignment, his reputation, his desired outcome.

The pairing is striking. One parsha opens with a law we can't understand. The other opens with a prophet who can't see. Both are about the edges of human sight.

I think the parshiot, read together, offer a complex lesson about perception. There are things we can't see because they exceed reason. And there are things we can't see because we're too invested in a particular view.

The red heifer is the first kind. It's not that we're failing to perceive it; it's that the thing itself is structured in a way that resists comprehension. The harder you reason about it, the less it yields. The only way to engage with it is through practice—through doing the ritual, through participation, through trust.

Balaam's angel is the second kind. It's not beyond perception; it's just outside his current frame. The donkey sees it because she has no frame. She's walking down a road. An obstacle appears. She avoids it. Her clear sight comes from her lack of agenda.

Put these together, and you get a picture of how human beings fail to see.

Some things exceed us. No matter how smart, how educated, how well-trained, we won't fully understand. The response to these things isn't to pretend we understand, or to reject them because we don't. The response is humility and practice.

And some things are blocked by us. Not by their complexity, but by our investment. We can't see what the donkey sees because we're too committed to where we're going. The response to these things isn't more expertise. It's less agenda. It's the willingness to stop and look at what actually is, not what we want to be there.

Both parshiot, in their way, say: *there is more than what you can see*. The Torah is insisting on this across two very different stories. In one, the thing you can't see is the logic of a law. In the other, it's an angel in the road.

What the two have in common is the call to humility. To remember that perception is partial. That what seems obvious might not be. That the inexplicable might still be true, and that what you're not looking for might still be there.

There's also something practical in this pairing. Most of what we miss in our lives falls into one of these two categories. Either it's something we don't have the framework to understand—something operating on a logic we haven't yet accessed. Or it's something we're not seeing because our attention is captured elsewhere.

The first kind, we meet with patience. We don't insist on understanding what isn't ours to understand. We do the practice. We trust the process. We remain open.

The second kind, we meet with humility. We recognize that our sight is skewed by our desires. We slow down. We ask what we might be missing. We let the donkey speak.

## What We Carry Forward

The things you can't see in your life fall into two categories. Some are beyond your reasoning—and the answer there is to participate, not to figure out. Some are in front of you but blocked by your own agenda—and the answer there is to stop, look, and let what's actually there become visible. This week, practice both. Some things you just do. Some things you just see.
