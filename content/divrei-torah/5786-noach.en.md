The flood is ending. Noach sends out a dove, and when it comes back, it's carrying something in its beak — a single olive leaf.

> **וְהִנֵּה עֲלֵה־זַיִת טָרָף בְּפִיהָ** &middot; *V'hinei aleh-zayit taraf b'fiha* &middot; "And behold, a plucked olive leaf in its mouth" (Bereishit 8:11).

Not a branch. A leaf. That always struck me as a little underwhelming. You'd think after forty days of rain, after the whole world has been remade, the dove would come back with something more decisive. A whole olive branch would be more like it. Instead, one leaf.

The Gemara in Eruvin has a line about this that changes the whole scene for me. The dove is basically saying: I'd rather have bitter food from God's hand than sweet food from a person's. She'd been fed well in the ark the whole time. Now she's picking something bitter and small — but it's the real thing.

And I think that's why the sign is a leaf and not a branch. Because a lot of the time in real life, that's how good news actually shows up. Not as a branch. As a leaf.

We tend to wait for the branch. We want the dramatic sign, the clear turning point, the moment where you can say "okay, we're through the worst of it." And while we're waiting for that, we miss a lot of leaves.

After a hard stretch, the first real sign that things are turning is almost never big. It's a decent conversation. A quiet morning. A day you didn't feel stuck. Something small, even a little bitter, but honest. That's usually what it actually looks like.

And that's what the Torah is pointing at here. Noach is watching the world come back to life, and the Torah is saying — pay attention to the leaf. Don't wait for the branch.

## Takeaway

Most good news shows up as a leaf, not a branch. When something small and honest happens this week — notice it. That's probably the sign.
