After Moshe comes down from Har Sinai the first time, he sees the golden calf, and he breaks the first set of tablets. He throws them to the ground and they shatter at the foot of the mountain.

He goes back up. God gives him a second set. He comes down with those.

And then the Torah says something that's often overlooked. The broken pieces — the shattered fragments of the first set — are not thrown away. They're gathered up and placed in the aron, the ark, alongside the second whole set. The broken and the whole travel together for the rest of the journey.

> **אֲשֶׁר שִׁבַּרְתָּ וְשַׂמְתָּם בָּאָרוֹן** &middot; "...which you broke — and you shall place them in the ark" (Devarim 10:2, referring back to this moment).

The Gemara in Bava Batra draws a line from this: *luchot v'shivrei luchot munachim ba'aron* — "the tablets and the broken pieces of the tablets rest together in the ark." Both are kept. Both are sacred. Neither is discarded.

This is not a small detail. The logic of it is almost offensive to how we usually think. You'd expect: we failed at Sinai the first time, Moshe broke the tablets in anger, we got new ones, end of story. Throw out the old ones, they're damaged goods. But the Torah insists the broken pieces travel inside the most sacred object we have, at the center of the camp, for the next forty years.

The message, if you sit with it, is extraordinary. What you broke is not erased by what came after. The failures, the false starts, the things that shattered — the Torah wants them carried. Wants them inside the holiest place. Wants them alongside the whole thing, not instead of it.

We tend to think that wholeness requires forgetting the broken parts. A spiritual life, a real life, a relationship — these get repaired and we try to put the brokenness away. The Torah puts it in the ark.

Because the broken pieces are not the opposite of holiness. They're part of how you got here. A person who has only ever been whole has not really lived. A person who has only been broken is a person who has not recovered. But a person who has been broken and put the pieces in the ark — that person is carrying a full Torah.

## Takeaway

Whatever broke in your life, before it got to where it is now — don't throw it away. The Torah kept the shattered tablets in the holiest place it had. You're allowed to carry your broken pieces in the same place as your whole ones. That's what an ark is for.
