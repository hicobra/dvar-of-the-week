The fourth book of the Torah opens with a strange name. *Bamidbar.* "In the wilderness."

Not "The Book of Numbers," which is what the English tradition calls it. Not "The Book of the Census," which would be more accurate to its opening content. *In the wilderness.* The Hebrew names its setting, not its subject.

And the setting matters. The whole book takes place in the desert—that in-between space between leaving Egypt and arriving in the land. A space that wasn't supposed to last as long as it did.

The Midrash asks: why did God give the Torah in the wilderness? Why not wait until the people arrived in the land, in a city, in a settled place? And the answer it gives is that the wilderness is nobody's. It doesn't belong to any tribe, any kingdom, any owner. The Torah had to be given in a place that wasn't owned, so that no one could claim a special relationship to it based on where they lived.

The wilderness equalizes. You can't pretend to be established in the wilderness. You can't build a permanent structure. You can't settle. Everyone is a stranger. Everyone is dependent.

There's something humbling about that. And there's something clarifying about it too. In the wilderness, the things that usually distinguish people—status, property, inheritance—mostly don't apply. What you have is what you can carry.

The book opens with a census. God tells Moses to count the people. Every man over twenty, tribe by tribe, name by name. It takes up the first chapter and a half. Long, dry, and precise. Numbers. Hence the English name.

But notice what the counting is *for*. It's not for taxation. It's not for bureaucracy. It's to arrange the camp. Each tribe has a specific place around the Mishkan. The Mishkan is in the center. The tribes radiate outward in a specific order. The camp is an ordered thing, but the order is organized around the center—around the place where God's presence rests.

So the wilderness isn't just wilderness. It's a wilderness with a center. An unmapped place with a precisely placed Mishkan. Everyone knows where they stand. Everyone knows what they're encamped around.

This is actually, I think, what the wilderness has to teach. It strips away everything external. And in the stripping away, it reveals what the actual center is. When you can't distinguish yourself by your house or your land or your wealth, you have to know what you're organized around.

Most of our lives, we're surrounded by infrastructure that lets us avoid that question. We have routines, possessions, careers, relationships that fill our days. We don't have to ask what's at the center because the scaffolding around us is so elaborate that the center is invisible.

But periodically, most of us get a kind of wilderness experience. A season where the usual structures don't work. A loss, a move, an illness, a change that strips the scaffolding. And in that wilderness, the question asserts itself: what am I actually organized around? What's in the middle of my camp?

The book of Bamidbar is going to follow the people for forty years through this wilderness. They'll fail and succeed. They'll lose their way and find it. But the structure never changes: there's always a center, and everyone is arranged around it.

## What We Carry Forward

Your life has a center, even if you don't notice it. This week, pay attention to what actually holds the camp together when the external structures fall away. That's your center. Make sure it's something worth standing around.
