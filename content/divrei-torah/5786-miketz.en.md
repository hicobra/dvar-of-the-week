For two full years, Yosef has been forgotten in prison. The butler promised to remember him. He didn't. Yosef sits.

And then Pharaoh has a dream, and everything turns over in a single day.

> **וַיִּשְׁלַח פַּרְעֹה וַיִּקְרָא אֶת־יוֹסֵף וַיְרִיצֻהוּ מִן־הַבּוֹר** &middot; *Vayishlach Pharaoh vayikra et Yosef vayritzuhu min habor* &middot; "And Pharaoh sent and called Yosef, and they rushed him out of the pit" (Bereishit 41:14).

Look at the pacing of that verse. *Vayishlach. Vayikra. Vayritzuhu.* Three consecutive verbs, all moving. They're not walking Yosef out. They're running him out. Get him a haircut. Get him new clothes. Pharaoh is waiting.

And by the end of the day, Yosef is the second most powerful man in Egypt.

Two years of nothing. And then, in a single day, everything.

The Sfas Emes notices this shift and says something worth sitting with. Redemption, when it comes, almost always comes faster than the trouble did. The trouble took years to set in. The redemption takes hours. That's just the Torah's pattern. Yosef in prison: two years. Yosef's rise to power: the course of one morning. Yetziat Mitzrayim: hundreds of years of slavery, and then *chipazon* — it all happens so fast the bread doesn't have time to rise.

Why does the Torah insist on this? Because the trouble shapes us in ways that feel permanent. When you've been stuck for long enough, it starts to feel like stuck is the new normal. Like this is just who you are now, and any change is going to require years of slow inching back out.

And the Torah says no. That's not how redemption works. When the turn comes, it comes fast. Faster than you think it can.

This doesn't mean the turn comes when we want it to. Yosef waited two years past when he expected to be out. The rescue was delayed. But when it arrived, it didn't unspool slowly to match the delay. It arrived all at once.

I think this is actually a kind of comfort. If you've been waiting a long time for something to turn, and you're quietly dreading how long the turn itself will take — you're probably overestimating it. The turn tends to take an afternoon.

## Takeaway

The trouble took years. The redemption usually takes a day. If something's been slow to change, don't assume the change itself will also be slow. When the turn comes, it tends to come fast.
