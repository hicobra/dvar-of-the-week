The first major set of laws after the Ten Commandments is mostly about everyday life — damages, theft, contracts. The very first law is about how to treat a Hebrew slave.

> **וְאִם־אָמֹר יֹאמַר הָעֶבֶד אָהַבְתִּי אֶת־אֲדֹנִי... וְרָצַע אֲדֹנָיו אֶת־אָזְנוֹ בַּמַּרְצֵעַ וַעֲבָדוֹ לְעֹלָם** &middot; "And if the servant shall say, 'I love my master'... his master shall pierce his ear with an awl, and he shall serve him forever" (Shemot 21:5–6).

A Hebrew slave goes free after six years. That's the rule. But if he *chooses* to stay — "I love my master" — the Torah commands that his ear be pierced against the doorpost. It's a strange ritual, and a strange image. Why the ear?

Rashi quotes a famous teaching from Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai. The ear, he says, is the organ that heard at Sinai — *"לֹא תִגְנֹב"*, "do not steal" — and this person, who has chosen to remain a slave when God declared all of us free, *didn't really hear*. So the ear — the instrument that was present for freedom and somehow missed it — is the part that gets marked.

What's sharp about this is who's being critiqued. The slave isn't being punished. He's not being shamed. The Torah lets him stay, if that's what he wants. But it marks him — marks his ear specifically — because something about the message of freedom passed through his ear and didn't change him.

The ear heard and didn't hear. That's the indictment.

I think about this a lot. There are things all of us have "heard" many times — at school, at seder, at shiurim, from parents, from our own experience — that haven't actually moved us. Facts we know. Values we agree with. Instructions we've received. But the hearing was only at the level of the ear. It never reached the feet.

Rabbi Yochanan's point is that this gap — between the ear and the rest of us — is a serious thing. It's serious enough that the Torah takes the unusual step of physically marking it. A man who hears "you are free" and stays a slave because he prefers the security of it hasn't just made a choice. He has demonstrated that his ear and his life are disconnected. The piercing is the Torah's way of saying: this matters. Close the gap.

## Takeaway

There's something you've heard many times that you know in your ears but haven't let into the rest of you. This week, pick one — and let it reach further than the ear.
