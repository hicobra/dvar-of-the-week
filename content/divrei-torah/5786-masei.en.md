The parsha opens with a list.

> **These are the journeys of the children of Israel, who went forth out of the land of Egypt by their armies under the hand of Moses and Aaron.** (Numbers 33:1)

What follows is the whole itinerary. Forty years. Every stop they made. From Egypt to the Jordan. Each place named. Each leg of the journey listed.

It's an exhaustive catalog. Forty-two stops. Most of them just names. *They set out from X and camped at Y. They set out from Y and camped at Z.* On and on.

Why is the Torah doing this? Why list every camp? We've been through the story already—all the crises, all the miracles, all the rebellions. Why recount the trip in this flat, bureaucratic form?

Rashi gives a beautiful answer. He says: God is walking through the journey one more time, before they enter the land, as a way of saying—look how far you've come. Look at every place. Look at every stop. None of it was wasted. None of it was random. This was the path.

Forty years is a long time. Most of them, as the Torah tells it, were spent wandering. The people had been ready to enter the land after two years. But after the spies came back with their discouraging report, the entry was delayed. A whole generation had to die out before the next generation could go in.

Those forty years must have felt, to those living them, like wasted time. Like a punishment that just kept going. Like they'd been consigned to an endless detour.

And yet the Torah, at the end, lists every stop. Not dismissively. Carefully. *And they set out from Refidim and camped in the wilderness of Sinai.* Each move preserved. Each camp honored. The whole itinerary remembered.

What this suggests to me is that the wandering wasn't wasted, even though it felt wasted. It was the journey. It had to happen. The people who emerged at the end of forty years were not the people who had started. They had been formed by the wandering. The itinerary was part of what made them capable of entering the land.

We tell our own stories this way, I think. Usually we remember the peaks. The moments when something important happened. The stops that felt significant. We don't usually catalog the in-between times—the stretches of unremarkable, apparently pointless going-through-the-motions.

But the Torah is saying: those are part of it too. Not just the dramatic moments. Not just the big events. The small encampments. The uneventful stops. The periods where nothing happened except that you were still walking.

There's a rabbinic teaching that each of the forty-two journeys has a counterpart in a person's life. Forty-two stages through which each soul passes. I don't know that I can map my life onto forty-two exact stops. But I recognize the idea. A life has stages. Some are recognizable. Some are forgettable. Each one, though, is a piece of what you've traveled.

When you look back, the goal is not to erase the unmemorable parts. It's to see them as the full journey. The apparent detours. The stops that didn't seem to matter. The years that felt lost. The Torah lists them all, with equal weight, because they were all part of the path to the land.

This is a kind of grace, I think. The willingness to count even the unremarkable days. To say: yes, I was there. I camped there. I set out from there. I kept going. It wasn't wasted.

## What We Carry Forward

Much of your life has felt like unremarkable walking. Stops you can barely remember. Seasons that seemed to go nowhere. This week, try to list them. Not as failures. As journeys. You were there. You kept going. None of it was wasted. That's the end of the book.
