Yitzchak is the quiet patriarch. Between the fire of Avraham and the storms of Yaakov, Yitzchak is the one you almost don't notice. He doesn't travel much. He doesn't wrestle angels. He doesn't build new altars — at least not many.

What he does do, repeatedly, is re-dig his father's wells.

> **וַיָּשָׁב יִצְחָק וַיַּחְפֹּר אֶת־בְּאֵרֹת הַמַּיִם אֲשֶׁר חָפְרוּ בִּימֵי אַבְרָהָם אָבִיו** &middot; "And Yitzchak returned and dug the wells of water that they had dug in the days of Avraham his father" (Bereishit 26:18).

After Avraham died, the Philistines filled all his wells with dirt. And Yitzchak goes, and he digs them back out, one by one. The Torah adds something easy to skim past: he gives each well "the same name his father gave them."

Why tell us the naming detail? Why not just say he re-dug them?

Because Yitzchak wasn't starting over. He wasn't trying to make his own mark. He wasn't trying to prove he was a new kind of patriarch. His job was *continuation*.

Some generations are called to dig new wells. Avraham dug new wells. Yaakov will build a whole new kind of family. But in between those two — there's a generation whose whole job is to hold the line. To make sure what was dug before doesn't get filled back in.

This isn't a small job. It might be the hardest job. It's much easier to start something new. Starting something new gets applause. Starting something new has a clear story. Re-digging a well someone else dug, and giving it the same name your father gave it — that's a life of quiet work with no obvious heroism.

And yet without Yitzchak, nothing continues. Avraham's wells would be dirt. The story would stop.

I think there are seasons of life, and sometimes even whole lives, that look like Yitzchak's. You're not breaking new ground. You're making sure old ground doesn't get lost. That's not a failure of imagination. That's a specific kind of avodah, and the Torah quietly gives it a whole Parsha.

## Takeaway

Some work is starting something new. Some work is making sure what's already been dug doesn't get filled in. Both are holy. If you're in a season of continuation and not of beginnings — don't mistake it for being stuck.
