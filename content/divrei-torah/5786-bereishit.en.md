Something I only started noticing recently: in the first chapter of the Torah, God keeps stopping to look. Six days of creation, and at the end of almost every day, it says the same thing — God saw that it was good.

> **וַיַּרְא אֱלֹהִים כִּי־טוֹב** &middot; *Vayar Elokim ki tov* &middot; "And God saw that it was good." It shows up seven times in the first chapter (Bereishit 1).

Seven times. That's not a passing detail. The Sforno picks up on it. He says the pause — the looking, the calling it good — is part of creating. The thing isn't really made until someone sees it and says it's good.

Which is a strange idea if you sit with it. You'd think creation ends the moment God speaks and the thing exists. But the Torah is saying — no. It ends when someone looks at it and calls it good.

And once you see this pattern, you can't unsee it. Make, then see. Make, then see. Six days in a row. And then day seven comes, and there's nothing left to make. The whole day is just looking.

That's a different way to think about Shabbat. It's not only a break because we're worn out. It's the day that completes the week. Without it, the week isn't done.

It's easy to let a week just end. You finish Friday, you light candles, you move on to the next thing. Work runs into Erev Shabbat, Erev Shabbat runs into Shabbat, and we never really stop to notice what the week was. But the Torah is saying — before you move on, stop. Look at what the week was. The conversations. The work. The small things that went right. Call it good.

Nothing dramatic. Just the same thing God does seven times on the first page.

## Takeaway

Before you light candles tonight, try it once. Stop. Look at your week. Say it was good. That's what the day is actually for.
