Yehuda walks up to Yosef.

We know Yosef is Yosef, the viceroy of Egypt, about to imprison Binyamin and send the rest of the brothers home to their father. They don't know. To them, he's a terrifying Egyptian official holding their youngest brother hostage.

And the parsha opens with this.

> **וַיִּגַּשׁ אֵלָיו יְהוּדָה וַיֹּאמֶר בִּי אֲדֹנִי** &middot; *Vayigash elav Yehuda vayomer bi adoni* &middot; "And Yehuda approached him and said, 'please, my lord'" (Bereishit 44:18).

The whole parsha is named after one word: *vayigash*. He approached. He walked forward.

Not "he begged." Not "he cried out." Not "he fell down at his feet." He *approached*. Something in the posture matters so much that the parsha takes its name from it.

Yehuda is about to give one of the most powerful speeches in the Torah. It's a speech that breaks Yosef open and ends a twenty-year rift. But the Torah doesn't start the parsha with the speech. It starts with the single step that came before the speech. Yehuda walks closer.

A midrash says Yehuda was ready to go to war, ready to beg, ready to do anything to protect Binyamin. But what he actually did first was walk forward. He closed the distance.

I keep noticing how often the hardest moments in our own lives ask this exact thing. There's someone you need to speak to. Something that needs to be said. A conversation that's been waiting for weeks. You can pray about it. You can rehearse it. You can write it out in your head. But there's a moment that precedes all of that, and it's the vayigash moment: you walk closer. You step toward the person. You take the physical step that signals you're not going to stay on the other side of the room anymore.

Nothing gets resolved before that step. And once you take it, it doesn't guarantee the outcome you're hoping for. But it's the precondition. The Torah names a whole parsha after it.

There's something hopeful in that. The hardest part is often not the speech. It's the step before the speech.

## Takeaway

The conversation you've been avoiding doesn't need the right words yet. It needs the step that comes before the words. This week, close the distance on the one that's been waiting.
