The parsha opens with a single word: *Emor*. Speak. Say.

And God tells Moses to speak to Aaron and to the people. The parsha is full of instructions that need to be communicated. Laws about priestly purity, rules about holy days, details about the Temple service, specifications for the menorah.

But there's something in the act of *saying* these things that matters more than the content of what's said. Moses is told to be the messenger. The message goes through him to Aaron and then to the people. The transmission is as much the point as the content.

The parsha includes the laws of the major festivals: Passover, Shavuot, Sukkot. These are not just things that happen; they're things that are *proclaimed*. The priest declares the holy day. The time is announced. The community gathers because the word has been spoken.

In our time, we often think of information as content that can be transmitted any way—written, posted, texted, whatever. The medium doesn't matter; the message does. The parsha suggests something different: that *saying* something, speaking it aloud, proclaiming it—these are acts with power in themselves.

When a day is proclaimed holy, it becomes holy not through some supernatural transformation, but through the community gathering and acknowledging the proclamation. The word creates the reality. Or, perhaps more accurately, the community's *hearing* and *heeding* the word creates the reality.

The parsha includes specific instructions about what counts as a holy day, how it's to be observed, what work is forbidden. But the prerequisite is always the proclaiming. Someone has to say it. Someone has to hear it said. The word has to move from the speaker to the listener, and only then does the observance follow.

This is why the parsha is called *Emor*—Speak. It's not called "The Laws of the Festivals." It's called by its first command: Go speak. Go say these things. The act of communication is primary.

In a literate age, we tend to think the written word is more reliable than the spoken word. A text doesn't change. A text has authority. Speech is ephemeral. But the Torah structure suggests that speech—direct, personal, accountable—is the primary mode of transmission.

The word comes from mouth to ear. From speaker to listener. In that act of direct address, something happens that reading alone doesn't quite achieve. The listener knows this is for them. The speaker takes responsibility for the words. There's an encounter.

We've lost some of this. We post important information and hope people read it. We broadcast messages and can't tell if anyone heard. We communicate but without the accountability that comes from saying something *to* someone, directly and in their presence.

The parsha's insistence on *Emor*—on the act of speaking—reminds us that communication is not just information transfer. It's an act of transmission that requires both a speaker and a listener, and it creates something between them. A covenant, maybe. An agreement that this has been said and heard.

The festivals that the parsha commands are not private practices. They're communal occasions that are marked by the declaration, by the word spoken. Someone says: "This is a holy day." And then the community, having heard, honors it.

We could just decide, privately, that certain days are important to us. But there's something stronger about the public declaration. About the priest standing and proclaiming it. About hearing the word spoken in community.

The parsha puts this at the center: Before the laws can be lived, the word must be spoken. Before the commandments can bind, they must be said. The *emor*—the speaking—comes first.

## What We Carry Forward

Is there something you need to say aloud—not post, not text, not write—but actually *say* to someone, face to face? The parsha says that the power is in the speaking. In the word moving between two people. Do it this week. Speak something true that needs to be heard.
