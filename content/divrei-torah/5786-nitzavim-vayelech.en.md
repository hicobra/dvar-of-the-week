When Nitzavim and Vayelech are read together, the two verbs at the heart of each parsha create a striking pair.

*Nitzavim.* Standing. The whole community assembled, upright, present, receiving the covenant.

*Vayelech.* He went. Moses walking, literally, among the people. Making his rounds. Beginning his handoff.

Standing and walking. Two different postures. And together, they describe something about how a person lives within a covenant.

You stand. You receive. You hear. You accept what is being offered to you.

And then you walk. You move. You carry what you've received into your actual life, into the days, into the places you go.

Neither posture alone is sufficient.

A person who only stands becomes a spectator. They receive the Torah and then... they just keep standing. Waiting for more instruction. Waiting for the perfect conditions. Waiting for the big moment. The covenant becomes a kind of museum piece—venerated, protected, displayed. But never actually lived.

A person who only walks, without ever standing to receive, becomes disoriented. They're in motion, but without a center. Busy, but not formed. Active, but not rooted.

The rhythm of the religious life, the parshiot together suggest, is stand and walk. Receive and carry. Accept and move.

Moses, at the end of his life, does both. He stands with the people to affirm the covenant. And then he walks among them, preparing the handoff, teaching the song, making sure what he has given will continue.

There's something poignant in this. Moses, who has led for forty years, still does both. He doesn't feel exempt from standing just because he's the teacher. He doesn't feel exempt from walking just because he's at the end.

Both postures are required, even at the end. Maybe especially at the end.

And the community is called to both as well. *You are standing today*, says Nitzavim. *Moses went*, says Vayelech. The stand and the walk.

This is how a covenant actually functions in a human life. You stand, every so often, and renew it. You remind yourself of what you've committed to. You let yourself be gathered with others who are standing in the same place. There's a seriousness and a stillness to the standing.

And then you walk. You go about your life. You make a living. You raise children or not. You do your work. You help someone. You make a mistake. You apologize. You wake up the next day and do it again. The walk is where the covenant actually plays out.

If you only ever stand, without walking, the covenant becomes abstract. If you only ever walk, without standing, the covenant gets lost in the noise of your life.

The rhythm, in Jewish practice, has been built into the calendar. Shabbat is a weekly standing. The holidays are annual standings. Each morning's prayer is a small standing. And in between, you walk.

You stand, briefly. You receive. You remember. And then you walk, as far and as long as the walking takes you—until the next standing.

Moses lived this pattern, and he modeled it. Even at the very end of his life, he stood *and* walked. He didn't stop.

And when he finally stopped, when he handed over to Joshua, he made sure the pattern would continue. Joshua would stand with the people. Joshua would walk with the people. The covenant would continue through a new teacher, in the same rhythm.

## What We Carry Forward

You need both postures this week. Stand somewhere, briefly, to remember what you're doing your life for. A moment of stillness. A moment of receiving. And then walk. Carry what you received into the week. Don't become a spectator. Don't become a blur. Both. Stand and walk.
