The parsha opens with instructions to Aaron about the menorah. *When you raise the lamps.* That's what the word *behaalotcha* means. When you kindle. When you raise the light.

Rashi asks a small question about this. Why "raise"? Why not "light"? You don't *raise* a lamp. You *light* it.

His answer is beautiful. He says: you raise the lamps because you hold the flame to the wick until the flame *rises on its own*. You don't force the lamp to burn. You don't push the fire into it. You stand there, patiently, until the lamp takes the flame—until the lamp rises into its own burning.

This is such a small detail, and such a large image. The priest isn't making the lamps burn. He's giving them the flame and waiting until they rise.

I think about this a lot in terms of teaching and parenting and mentoring. We sometimes think our job is to force something into someone. To push knowledge or wisdom or values into them. To make them burn. To light them up.

But Rashi suggests the real work is different. The real work is to hold the flame steady—to stay present with patience—until the other person catches fire on their own.

You can't rush this. You can't force it. You can stand there too briefly and fail to give the flame enough time. You can also stand there pushing, trying to make it happen, which usually just blows the flame out.

The priest holds the flame. The lamp takes it. And then the flame rises on its own.

There's something humbling about this view of Aaron's job. The priest isn't the source of the light. He's just the one who holds the flame close enough, long enough, for the lamp to take it.

The parsha continues with other stories—the complaints of the people, the sending of spies, Miriam speaking against Moses. But the opening image—the patient priest, holding the flame, waiting for the lamps to rise—stays with me as the heart of the parsha.

Aaron, in the rabbinic tradition, is associated with peace. He's the great peacemaker, the one who would go between two people in conflict and get them to reconcile. He's also, here, the one who lights the menorah. Perhaps these are related. Perhaps peacemaking, too, is a kind of patient holding-of-flame. You can't force two people to be reconciled. You can only hold a steady flame between them and wait until they catch it on their own.

The menorah was the light source in the Mishkan, and later in the Temple. It wasn't functional lighting in the modern sense—it was symbolic. It said: in the holy place, light is important. It's the first thing Aaron does in the morning. It's the last thing he tends to at night.

And the light in the holy place wasn't abundant—there were seven lamps, not a hundred. It was intentional, specific, carefully tended. Each lamp mattered. Each one had to be raised into its burning.

This might be the image: not that you flood a space with light, but that you tend the lights you have. Seven flames. Seven careful risings. Enough light for the work. And each flame matters, because the priest had to stand with each one until it rose.

## What We Carry Forward

You're probably trying to make something happen that actually needs to happen on its own. This week, try Aaron's patience. Hold your flame near. Give the other lamp time to rise into its own burning. Don't leave too soon. And don't push.
